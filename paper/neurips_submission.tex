\begin{abstract}
Auxiliary losses often stabilize representation learning, yet
sometimes degrade late-training performance in ways that look like
ordinary RL noise. We identify a learning-dynamics mechanism
governing this failure: when auxiliary targets are both
\emph{structured} (coupled to the current task state) and
\emph{non-stationary} (drifting as other learners update), they
inject directional gradient noise that dominates policy gradients
near convergence. The effect is not magnitude imbalance, not
multi-task conflict, and not capacity consumption; it is driven by
target drift across learners. We test this principle in cooperative MARL
where co-learning teammates provide the natural non-stationarity.
A target-source distinguishing experiment holds aux capacity,
architecture, and the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway fixed and
varies only the target: co-learning teammate targets are
pathological ($463 \pm 9$, $d = +1.40$ vs No Aux with bootstrap CI
above zero); frozen-initial-policy targets (structured but stationary)
and uniform random targets (unstructured, stationary) are both
not resolved from the No Aux regime at $n{=}5$ (CIs cross zero).
A complementary $8\times$ aux-capacity sweep has no effect, jointly
inconsistent with capacity consumption as the driver. Supervised
CIFAR-100 defines the principle's boundary: zero effect, as the
theory predicts.
Effect size scales with coordination complexity across four MARL
benchmarks (Hanabi, Cramped Room, AA, SMAX) and with target-policy
drift rate, as the principle predicts. Three
gradient-pathway interventions recover the no-aux regime on
Overcooked. All 375{+} runs and code at
\url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/aux-loss-considered-harmful}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Auxiliary prediction losses are ubiquitous in modern deep learning:
UNREAL~\cite{jaderberg2016reinforcement}, world
models~\cite{hafner2023mastering}, self-predictive
representations~\cite{schwarzer2021dataefficient}, and the MARL
belief-learning pattern of Dynamic Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic} and
BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal} all attach a constant-weight auxiliary
loss to a shared encoder. Most of the time it helps. Sometimes it
silently corrupts late-training convergence with cross-seed variance
that looks like ordinary RL noise. This paper identifies the
learning-dynamics principle that separates the two regimes.

\emph{\textbf{Principle (informal).} Auxiliary learning fails most
severely when targets are both structured and co-adaptive, because
the resulting gradient direction becomes a non-stationary process
that overwhelms the vanishing policy gradient near convergence;
removing either ingredient reduces the effect.}

\textbf{What it is, what it is not.} The failure mechanism is
\emph{directional}, not magnitude-based: gradient-surgery methods
that rebalance magnitudes (PCGrad~\cite{yu2020gradient},
GradNorm~\cite{chen2018gradnorm}) do not address it. It is not
multi-task conflict, because a single auxiliary is enough. And it is
not capacity consumption, because an $8\times$ sweep of aux capacity
has no effect. What matters is the \emph{target}: when it drifts
with another learner's updates, the aux-gradient direction becomes
a non-stationary process that shrinking policy gradients near
convergence cannot dominate. Table~\ref{tab:contrast} locates this
claim relative to prior work.

\textbf{Cooperative MARL as test bed.} Co-learning teammates are a
clean natural source of structured non-stationary targets: a
teammate-action-prediction aux target is coupled to the game state
\emph{and} drifts as teammates update. Zhai et
al.~\cite{zhai2023dynamic}'s Static-Belief ablation reports exactly
this pattern (Traffic Junction success $73.4\% \to 71.9\%$); we
characterize the mechanism behind their observation. We construct
\textbf{VABL} (\S\ref{sec:background}) as a minimal architecture in
this class and test the principle with three experiments.
\emph{(i) Target-source distinguishing test} (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}):
the principle predicts the ordering random $<$ frozen $<$ teammate in
pathology severity; the data confirm it. \emph{(ii) Aux-capacity
sweep}: $8\times$ variation has no effect. \emph{(iii) Boundary}:
pathology scales with coordination-induced teammate drift across four
MARL benchmarks; MPE's slow symmetric drift and supervised CIFAR-100
show zero effect as the principle predicts for stationary targets.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Principle.} A learning-dynamics failure mode:
structured, non-stationary auxiliary targets induce directional
gradient noise near convergence, with a testable ordering prediction
over target sources (random $<$ frozen $<$ co-learning).
\item \textbf{Causal identification.} A target-source intervention
and an $8\times$ aux-capacity sweep are together inconsistent with
generic capacity-consumption and multi-task-conflict explanations
in this setting.
\item \textbf{Theory.} A first-order model linking target-policy
drift to gradient noise via
$\Sigma_\varepsilon \approx J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top$
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:drift_to_noise}), with a drift-to-variance bound
and a cosine-std diagnostic that separates pathway-active from
pathway-zero conditions.
\item \textbf{Validation.} The predicted ordering and null conditions
hold across four cooperative MARL benchmarks, a supervised-stationary
boundary (CIFAR-100), and a slow-symmetric-drift boundary
(MPE simple\_spread).
\item \textbf{Practical fixes.} Three gradient-pathway interventions
($\lambda$-annealing, stop-gradient on the belief, critic-side aux
placement) recover the no-aux regime on Overcooked; SMAX retains a
residual mean gap with non-monotonic $\lambda$ response that we flag
as open.
\item \textbf{Benchmark.} A reproducible 375{+}-run matrix with 5
seeds and bootstrap CIs. As a secondary finding (\S\ref{sec:budget}),
much of the widely reported Overcooked coordination collapse is a
training-budget artifact distinct from our pathology.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Auxiliary losses in RL.}
UNREAL~\cite{jaderberg2016reinforcement} pioneered auxiliary tasks;
subsequent work uses world-model
losses~\cite{hafner2023mastering}, self-predictive
representations~\cite{schwarzer2021dataefficient,fang2024predictive},
and contrastive objectives~\cite{laskin2020curl}. Theoretical analyses
of auxiliary-task
dynamics~\cite{lyle2021effect,voelcker2024selfprediction} cover TD and
latent self-prediction; \cite{du2018adapting,lin2019adaptive} argue
constant weights are suboptimal.
\cite{moalla2024norepnotrust} document on-policy PPO representation
collapse under non-stationarity; \cite{chua2024successor} show
successor-feature losses collapse basis features to cosine-to-1,
resolved by stop-gradient in the reward loss (an analogue to our
stop-gradient fix path). In MARL,
XP-MARL~\cite{xu2024xpmarl} prioritizes training samples for
non-stationarity; BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal} and Dynamic
Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic} directly instantiate the design pattern
we study (attention-based belief encoder $+$ fixed-weight
teammate-future-prediction aux). Zhai et al.~\cite{zhai2023dynamic}'s
Static-Belief ablation (their Table~2) reports that naive co-training
drops Traffic Junction success from $73.4\%$ to $71.9\%$; their fix
is a meta-learned dynamic context, while we identify the mechanism
and show pathway-severing interventions suffice without
meta-learning. BEPAL's oracle (stationary) targets are consistent
with the principle. Contemporaneous~\cite{weng2026exploration}
identifies a distinct magnitude-driven pathology in parameter-shared
DQN.

\paragraph{Attention and stop-gradient.}
AERIAL~\cite{phan2023attention} uses QMIX-based attention over
teammate hidden states (no auxiliary head);
MAAC~\cite{iqbal2019actor} places attention in the centralized critic;
MAT~\cite{wen2022mat} uses an encoder-decoder for centralized policy
learning. None pair attention-based \emph{decentralized} belief
encoding with a constant-weight teammate-future-prediction aux loss.
In SSL, BYOL~\cite{grill2020bootstrap},
SimSiam~\cite{chen2021exploring}, and DINO~\cite{caron2021emerging} use
stop-gradient to prevent representation collapse; our stop-gradient
fix path is the MARL analogue.

\paragraph{Gradient interference and coordination collapse.}
PCGrad~\cite{yu2020gradient}, GradNorm~\cite{chen2018gradnorm},
HyperMARL~\cite{tessera2025hypermarl}, and multi-timescale
learning~\cite{nekoei2023dealing} project, reweight, or decouple
gradients by magnitude or across agents; direction-oriented
multi-objective
optimization~\cite{xiao2023sdmgrad,zhang2025mgda,patapati2025graphcoloring}
schedules by inter-task gradient conflict.
Cosine-based gradient-alignment diagnostics have appeared on
several axes: ROCKET~\cite{sun2026rocket} identifies cross-layer
gradient conflict in representation alignment and shows that a
shared (PSD-structured) projector eliminates it;
GAC~\cite{xu2026gac} uses consecutive-step gradient cosine as a
stability signal for asynchronous LLM RL; DG-PG~\cite{yang2026dgpg}
decomposes cooperative-MARL gradient variance into self versus
cross-agent components with closed-form variance-reduction
combining. Our setting is the temporal policy-vs-auxiliary axis
within a single agent. Offline
MARL~\cite{tilbury2024coordination} identifies a distinct directional
pathology from data-mean-biased best responses. Our pathology is
intra-agent, directional rather than magnitude-driven
(Figure~\ref{fig:gradients}c), and environment-dependent.
Overcooked~\cite{carroll2019utility} is widely studied with prior
reports of severe degradation~\cite{yu2022surprising,rashid2020monotonic};
recent benchmarking shows algorithm rankings depend sharply on
training budget~\cite{formanek2024mirage,papadopoulos2025extended,bettini2024benchmarl}.
At 10M steps most reported ``collapse'' is insufficient training; the
residual instability we identify is distinct and gradient-mediated.

\section{Background: Attention-Based Belief Learning}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================================

The design pattern we investigate, attention-based belief encoders
trained with constant-weight auxiliary prediction losses targeting
teammate future behavior, has recent instances in
BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal} and Dynamic
Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic}, and sits within the broader family of
constant-weight aux
architectures~\cite{hafner2023mastering,schwarzer2021dataefficient,jaderberg2016reinforcement}.
We construct \textbf{VABL} as a minimal cooperative
Dec-POMDP~\cite{oliehoek2016concise} instance of this class in which
attention, aux loss, and aux-to-encoder gradient flow can each be
manipulated independently.

\paragraph{Belief update.}
Each agent $i$ maintains a recurrent latent belief $b_t^i \in
\mathbb{R}^{d_h}$, updated at each timestep from (i) an observation
embedding $\phi_\theta(o_t^i) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ and (ii) a social
context vector $c_t^i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ aggregated from observable
teammate actions:
\begin{equation}
    b_t^i = \mathrm{GRU}\left(
        [\phi_\theta(o_t^i) \| c_t^i],\; b_{t-1}^i
    \right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{GRU}$ denotes a gated recurrent unit~\cite{cho2014gru}.

\paragraph{Attention context.}
The social context $c_t^i$ is computed via multi-head
attention~\cite{vaswani2017attention} over action embeddings of
visible teammates, with query $W_q b_{t-1}^i$ and keys/values
$\{\psi_\theta(a_{t-1}^j) + e_j\}_{j \neq i}$ (learnable identity
embeddings $e_j$); per-step visibility masks zero out invisible
teammates. The mean-pooling ablation replaces MHA with the
unweighted average.

\paragraph{Policy and auxiliary heads.}
The policy $\pi_\theta(a_t^i \mid b_t^i)$ is a linear head on the belief.
The auxiliary predictor $\hat{\pi}_\varphi(\cdot \mid b_t^i)$ is a 2-layer
MLP that predicts each visible teammate's next action from the current
belief, trained with cross-entropy:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}}^i = \mathbb{E}\left[
        \sum_{j \neq i} m_{t+1}^{i \leftarrow j}\,
        \mathrm{CE}(\hat{\pi}_\varphi(\cdot \mid b_t^i),\, a_{t+1}^j)
    \right].
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The total loss combines a PPO policy gradient~\cite{schulman2017ppo}, a centralized value loss, entropy
regularization, and the auxiliary loss:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PPO}} +
    c_v \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{value}} +
    c_e \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{entropy}} +
    \lambda \sum_i \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}}^i,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = 0.05$ is the auxiliary loss weight. In the standard
configuration, $\lambda$ is held constant throughout training.

\paragraph{Gradient pathway.} The auxiliary loss gradient flows
through the belief $b_t^i$ into the attention mechanism, GRU, and
observation encoder $\phi_\theta$. Section~\ref{sec:results} identifies
this pathway as the source of the late-training instability.

\section{Theory: From Teammate Drift to Directional Interference}
\label{sec:theory}
% ============================================================================

\emph{Intuitively, as policy gradients shrink near convergence, even
small fluctuations in auxiliary gradient direction can dominate
updates, turning convergence into a stochastic drift process.} We
derive a first-order mechanistic model of this effect in four steps:
(i) define directional interference and distinguish it from magnitude
interference; (ii) derive the aux-gradient noise covariance from
target-policy drift; (iii) bound late-training parameter variance
in terms of that covariance; (iv) state a threshold condition for
instability onset. The model connects co-learning dynamics to
observed instability through causally upstream quantities ($\Sigma_\pi$,
$J_\pi$) rather than treating gradient noise as exogenous, using
standard linearization steps we make explicit below.

\paragraph{Step 1: Defining directional interference.} Let
$g_\pi(t) = \nabla_\theta L_{\mathrm{PPO}}(\theta_t)$ and
$g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t) = \lambda \nabla_\theta L_{\mathrm{aux}}(\theta_t;
\pi_j^{(t)})$ be the two gradient signals the actor receives at
iteration $t$. Prior gradient-conflict work
(PCGrad~\cite{yu2020gradient}, GradNorm~\cite{chen2018gradnorm})
treats conflict as a \emph{magnitude} imbalance: persistent
$\mathbb{E}[g_\pi^\top g_{\mathrm{aux}}] < 0$ or
$\|g_{\mathrm{aux}}\| \gg \|g_\pi\|$. We define \emph{directional
interference} as temporal variance in gradient alignment:
\begin{equation}
    I_{\mathrm{dir}}
    = \mathrm{Var}_t\!\left[\tfrac{g_\pi(t)^\top g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t)}{\|g_\pi(t)\|\|g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t)\|}\right],
    \quad
    I_{\mathrm{mag}}
    = \left(\mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\tfrac{g_\pi(t)^\top g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t)}{\|g_\pi(t)\|\|g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t)\|}\right]\right)^2 .
    \label{eq:idir}
\end{equation}
High $I_{\mathrm{mag}}$ signals a persistent conflicting direction
(what PCGrad corrects). High $I_{\mathrm{dir}}$ with $I_{\mathrm{mag}}
\approx 0$ signals alignment that \emph{oscillates}: neither helpful
nor persistently harmful but corrosive to convergence.
Figure~\ref{fig:gradients} measures Full VABL's late-training signature
($\mathbb{E}[\cos] \approx 0$, $\mathrm{std}(\cos) = 0.185$), i.e.\
$I_{\mathrm{dir}} \gg I_{\mathrm{mag}}$.

\paragraph{Step 2: Deriving $\Sigma_\varepsilon$ from target-policy drift.}
Let $\pi_j \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\pi}$ denote the policy whose actions
define the aux label at step $t$; in Full VABL this is the teammate's
own co-learning policy, while the frozen-target and random-target
conditions (\S\ref{sec:method_distinguishing}) replace it with a
stationary alternative, so that $\Sigma_\pi$ and $J_\pi$ below
always refer to the target-generating policy rather than to any
other agent. Represent $\pi_j$ either by its parameters or,
equivalently in the linear regime, by its action-probability vector
stacked over the observed state distribution. Decompose the aux
gradient as $g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t) = \bar g_{\mathrm{aux}} +
\varepsilon(t)$. A first-order expansion of
$g_{\mathrm{aux}}$ around $\bar\pi_j$, the time-averaged target generating 
policy over the analysis window, gives
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon(t) \approx J_\pi \,(\pi_j^{(t)} - \bar\pi_j),
    \qquad
    \Sigma_\varepsilon \approx J_\pi \,\Sigma_\pi\, J_\pi^\top,
    \qquad
    \Sigma_\pi = \mathrm{Cov}_t[\pi_j^{(t)}],
    \label{eq:drift_to_noise}
\end{equation}
where $J_\pi \in \mathbb{R}^{|\theta| \times d_\pi} = \partial
g_{\mathrm{aux}}/\partial \pi_j |_{\bar\pi_j}$ and $J_\pi$ absorbs
the $\lambda$ scaling of $g_{\mathrm{aux}}$. The gradient noise
covariance factors through the target-generating policy's drift
covariance. Two
limits: a frozen target-generating policy ($\Sigma_\pi = 0$) gives
$\Sigma_\varepsilon \to 0$; a target constructed not to depend on
the target-generating policy ($J_\pi = 0$, e.g.\ random or
supervised targets) also gives $\Sigma_\varepsilon \to 0$. The
target-source distinguishing test (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}) realizes
both limits empirically. Equivalently, DG-PG's variance-reduction
framework for cooperative MARL~\cite{yang2026dgpg} assumes an
\emph{exogenous} guidance target, $\nabla_\theta \tilde{x} = 0$
(their Assumption~3.1); in our setting the aux target is generated
by a co-evolving policy (the teammate's in Full VABL), so this
exogeneity assumption is violated and $\Sigma_\varepsilon$ is
driven by $\Sigma_\pi$ rather than collapsing. The $\delta$-stationarity bound
of~\cite{li2022trustregion} formalizes $\Sigma_\pi$ via the
KL-divergence of consecutive joint policies.
The linearization is local in policy space and tightest in late
training, where convergence implies small per-iteration drift.

\paragraph{Step 3: Noise-driven parameter variance.}
Near a local optimum $\theta^*$ the policy loss is approximately
quadratic with Hessian $H \succ 0$, giving $\nabla_\theta
L_{\mathrm{PPO}} = H(\theta - \theta^*)$ which shrinks as $\theta \to
\theta^*$. Centering around $\theta^*_{\mathrm{eff}} = \theta^* -
H^{-1} \bar g_{\mathrm{aux}}$, the displacement $\delta(t) = \theta(t)
- \theta^*_{\mathrm{eff}}$ evolves as
\begin{equation}
    \delta(t{+}1) = (I - \alpha H)\,\delta(t) - \alpha\,\varepsilon(t).
    \label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Local drift-to-variance scaling]
\label{prop:variance}
Under Eq.~\eqref{eq:dynamics} with $\varepsilon(t)$ treated as
i.i.d.\ with covariance given by Eq.~\eqref{eq:drift_to_noise} and
spectral radius $\rho(I - \alpha H) < 1$, the steady-state
displacement variance satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\|\delta\|^2]
    \;\propto\;
    \frac{\alpha \,\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \,\Sigma_\pi\, J_\pi^\top)}
         {\lambda_{\min}(H)}.
    \label{eq:variance_bound}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

The bound gives the scaling \emph{shape}, not absolute magnitude:
colored $\varepsilon(t)$ inflates the right-hand side by an
autocorrelation-time factor $\tau_c$ without changing the
dependence on $\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top)$ or
$\lambda_{\min}(H)$.

\paragraph{Step 4: threshold and testable predictions.}
Let $\eta = \mathbb{E}_t[\|g_\pi(t)\|]$ denote the typical
policy-gradient magnitude; the same Lyapunov argument behind
Proposition~\ref{prop:variance} gives a policy-signal displacement
variance $\alpha \eta^2 / \lambda_{\min}(H)$. Instability onset
corresponds to the aux-drift variance exceeding this signal variance:
\begin{equation}
    \underbrace{\frac{\alpha \,\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \,\Sigma_\pi\, J_\pi^\top)}{\lambda_{\min}(H)}}_{\text{aux-drift variance}}
    \;\gtrsim\;
    \underbrace{\frac{\alpha \eta^2}{\lambda_{\min}(H)}}_{\text{policy-signal variance}},
    \label{eq:threshold}
\end{equation}
equivalently $\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top) \gtrsim
\eta^2$ after the common $\alpha/\lambda_{\min}(H)$ factor
cancels. Writing $J_\pi = \lambda \tilde J_\pi$ makes the
$\lambda$-scaling explicit: $\lambda^2 \mathrm{tr}(\tilde J_\pi
\Sigma_\pi \tilde J_\pi^\top) \gtrsim \eta^2$. This yields three
testable predictions:
\textbf{(a) Stationarity null}: $\Sigma_\pi \approx 0$ (supervised
labels, symmetric slow drift) $\Rightarrow$ no pathology.
\textbf{(b) Pathway null}: interventions that drive
$J_\pi \to 0$ in the encoder subspace (stop-gradient, critic-side
aux, annealed $\lambda$) $\Rightarrow$ recovery.
\textbf{(c) Target ordering}: ranking target sources by the
$J_\pi\!\cdot\Sigma_\pi$ coupling (random targets $\Rightarrow J_\pi
{=} 0$; frozen targets $\Rightarrow \Sigma_\pi {=} 0$; co-learning
$\Rightarrow$ both non-zero) predicts the pathology-severity ordering.
All three are tested in \S\ref{sec:mechanism}--\ref{sec:cross_env}.

\textbf{Scope of the $\lambda$ prediction.}
The bound predicts variance $\propto \lambda^2$ locally; our
3-point sweep is non-monotonic because $J_\pi$ itself co-adapts
with $\lambda$ at small $\lambda$ (Appendix~\ref{app:lambda}).
The prediction holds within a $\lambda$ range where aux-head
representations are qualitatively unchanged, not globally.

\paragraph{Interpreting $J_\pi$.}
$J_\pi$ is the sensitivity of the aux gradient to the target-policy
action distribution; its column space coincides with the pathway
through attention/GRU/$\phi_\theta$. It is large when the aux head
is tightly tuned to the current target and small when the encoder
is structurally insensitive (mean pooling, stop-gradient, or
critic-side placement).

\paragraph{From cosine to proxy.}
Linearizing $\cos(g_\pi, g_{\mathrm{aux}})$ at fixed norms,
$\mathrm{Var}_t[\cos] \approx \hat g_\pi^\top \Sigma_\varepsilon
\hat g_\pi / \|\bar g_{\mathrm{aux}}\|^2 \leq
\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\varepsilon)/\|\bar g_{\mathrm{aux}}\|^2$, so
the cosine temporal std is a finite-sample proxy for normalized
$\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top)^{1/2}$, measured on the
final $50\%$ window. GAC's consecutive-step cosine diagnostic in
async LLM RL~\cite{xu2026gac} is a related but distinct temporal
metric (single-stream autocorrelation, not policy-vs-aux alignment).
Figure~\ref{fig:proxy} shows $R^2 = 0.94$ across our four logged
conditions (three have proxy zero by construction, so the plot
verifies ordering more than slope). Within Full's 5 seeds,
per-seed cosine std correlates with per-seed peak-to-Final50 drop
at $r = +0.40$ (weak at $n{=}5$).

\paragraph{Drift-gated aux.}
Eq.~\eqref{eq:threshold} yields an online controller: gate
\texttt{stop\_gradient} on the aux-to-encoder pathway whenever a
sliding-window estimate $\hat s(t)$ of $\mathrm{std}(\cos)$ exceeds
$\tau$, interpolating between always-on and stop-gradient limits.
DG-PG~\cite{yang2026dgpg} (Theorem~4.2) gives a closed-form optimal
$\alpha^*(t)$ for combining $(g_\pi, g_{\mathrm{aux}})$ with
correlation $\rho = \mathbb{E}_t[\cos]$; our binary gating is a
coarse approximation. COALA-PG~\cite{meulemans2024coala} formalizes
the co-learning meta-POMDP underlying $\Sigma_\pi$. Empirical
evaluation of both the continuous schedule and the gated variant
is future work.

\paragraph{Scope and open questions.}
The first-order model gives the variance-scaling \emph{shape}, not
absolute magnitude: autocorrelated $\varepsilon(t)$ can inflate the
true variance by an $O(\tau_c)$ factor without changing sign. The
local-quadratic approximation applies most tightly in late training
where drift is small (also where the pathology is least severe).
The critic-side reversal we observe ($d = -0.79$,
Appendix~\ref{app:arch_sweep}) and the SMAX residual mean gap (fix
paths recover variance but not mean) fall outside the scope of
Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}; the latter is inconsistent with a
purely zero-mean noise mechanism and we flag it as open.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Ablation Matrix and Fix-Path Interventions}
We run VABL in a $2 \times 2$ ablation over attention vs.\ mean
pooling and auxiliary loss on ($\lambda = 0.05$ constant) vs.\ off,
yielding \textbf{Full} (attn+aux), \textbf{No Attention}, \textbf{No
Aux}, and \textbf{Neither}. Three gradient-only interventions on
Full: \textbf{Anneal} (linearly decay $\lambda$ from $0.05$ to $0$
over the first 50\% of training), \textbf{Stop-gradient}
(\texttt{stop\_gradient} on $b_t^i$ before the aux head; aux MLP
still trains but gradients do not reach the encoder), and
\textbf{Anneal + stop-gradient} (both combined).

\subsection{Target-Source Distinguishing Test}
\label{sec:method_distinguishing}

To distinguish directional interference from capacity consumption,
we run Full VABL with aux network, capacity, and gradient pathway
identical to Full, varying \emph{only} the target source:
\textbf{Frozen-policy targets} (argmax of an iteration-0 random-init
snapshot $\pi_{\theta_0}$: stationary but structured) and
\textbf{Uniform random targets} (resampled each iteration: stationary
and unstructured). Capacity consumption predicts both to be
pathological; directional interference predicts recovery scaled by
residual structure.

\subsection{Environments, baselines, and metrics}
Four JaxMARL-based~\cite{rutherford2024jaxmarl} cooperative MARL
environments (Overcooked~\cite{carroll2019utility} Asymmetric
Advantages and Cramped Room, Hanabi 2-player~\cite{bard2020hanabi},
MPE simple\_spread~\cite{lowe2017multi}, SMAX
3v3~\cite{samvelyan2019starcraft,ellis2023smacv2}) plus CIFAR-100
with a small ViT + RotNet aux head as a supervised-stationary
control. Baselines cover three paradigms (decentralized policy
gradient: MAPPO~\cite{yu2022surprising}; communication:
TarMAC~\cite{das2019tarmac}, CommNet~\cite{sukhbaatar2016commnet};
attention: AERIAL~\cite{phan2023attention}), reimplemented in JAX
with matched hyperparameters. Metrics are Best (peak episode
reward or test accuracy), Final50 (mean of last 50 episodes;
Final5 for vision), cross-seed std, and Cohen's
$d$~\cite{cohen1988statistical}; mean $\pm$ std over 5 seeds
throughout. Full environment specs, baseline description, and
metric definitions in Appendix~\ref{app:env_baselines_metrics}.

\subsection{Causal Identification of the Mechanism}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The fix paths are consistent with two distinct stories: directional
interference driven by structured non-stationary aux targets, and a
generic aux-capacity-consumption alternative in which any aux head
attached to the shared encoder degrades late-training performance
regardless of what the aux gradient encodes. Two independent
controls are inconsistent with the latter.

\textbf{Target-source test.} We hold aux capacity, architecture, and
the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway identical to Full and vary only
the \emph{source} of the aux target (\S\ref{sec:method_distinguishing}).
The capacity-consumption hypothesis predicts all three conditions to
be pathological. Table~\ref{tab:target_source} is inconsistent with
this: at $n{=}5$, teammate co-learning remains distinct from No Aux
($d{=}+1.40$, CI $[{+}0.47,{+}3.58]$), while both frozen-vs-No Aux
($d{=}-0.75$, CI $[-2.73,{+}0.44]$) and random-vs-No Aux ($d{=}-0.04$,
CI $[-1.47,{+}1.40]$) CIs cross zero (no resolved effect, not a
formal equivalence test). Structured co-learning targets carry
teammate-policy-specific information that drifts across iterations;
frozen targets remove the drift, random targets remove both. Only
the structured-and-drifting combination degrades Final50 stability.

\subsection{Generalization and budget artifact}
\label{sec:arch_sweep}
\label{sec:budget}

A 60-run architecture sweep and a 3-point $\lambda$ sweep confirm
generalization: the pathology reproduces with LSTM + cross-attention
($d{=}0.80$) and GRU + additive attention ($d{=}1.01$), is absent
under mean pooling ($d{=}-0.30$), reverses on critic-side aux
($d{=}-0.79$), and at 50M steps retains cross-seed std $>7$ at both
$\lambda{=}0.01$ and $\lambda{=}0.05$; a 6-method benchmark
separately shows the widely-reported coordination collapse on AA
drops from $\sim$46\% at 500K to $<$3\% at 10M steps, so our
residual instability is distinct from that budget-artifact collapse
(Appendices~\ref{app:arch_sweep},~\ref{app:lambda},~\ref{app:tables}).




% ============================================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================================

The model is first-order (local quadratic loss, i.i.d.\ noise,
linear expansion in $\pi_j$); $J_\pi$ and $\Sigma_\pi$ are not
directly observed but are proxied by the normalized cosine
temporal std (Figure~\ref{fig:proxy}). The bound is tightest in
late training; colored-noise and mid-training extensions are in
Appendix~\ref{app:theory_extended}. Two empirical patterns fall
outside Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}'s scope and we flag as
open: the critic-side aux reversal ($d = -0.79$,
Appendix~\ref{app:arch_sweep}) shows routing $g_{\mathrm{aux}}$
through the critic \emph{improves} stability, and on SMAX fix
paths recover variance but not the mean gap (inconsistent with a
purely zero-mean noise mechanism). 5 seeds per condition on all
MARL cells and CIFAR-100; the architecture sweep resolves at
positive $d$ in 2 of 4 configurations; absolute Hanabi scores are
low despite clean relative ordering. Empirical evaluation of the
drift-gated aux controller (\S\ref{sec:theory}) and of DG-PG's
closed-form schedule~\cite{yang2026dgpg} it approximates, along
with replication on BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal} and Dynamic
Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic}, are immediate future work.


% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================================

A constant auxiliary weight on a target that is both task-coupled
and co-adaptive is not a free regularizer; it is a silent
late-training stability risk whose severity scales with how fast
the target drifts relative to the vanishing policy gradient. When
either ingredient is absent, the aux is harmless.

More broadly, gradient conflict in deep policy learning is not
always a magnitude problem and not always a multi-task problem.
Directional, target-driven interference is a distinct failure mode
with its own diagnostic (temporal gradient-cosine variance) and
its own remedy (sever the encoder-side pathway). A drift-gated
aux controller approximating the DG-PG~\cite{yang2026dgpg}
closed-form weight is the natural successor; the critic-side
reversal and SMAX residual mean gap remain open.

\section{Extended Theory Discussion}
\label{app:theory_extended}

This appendix expands the compressed theory exposition in
\S\ref{sec:theory}. It is the long-form version of material
referenced but not stated in full in the main text, organized as
follows.
\S\ref{app:theory_linearization} specifies exactly when the
first-order linearization $\varepsilon(t) \approx J_\pi
(\pi_j^{(t)} - \bar\pi_j)$ is accurate, and why restricting the
analysis window to the final $50\%$ of training is the correct
regime for measuring the cosine-std proxy.
\S\ref{app:theory_colored} extends Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}
from the i.i.d.\ noise assumption used in the main body to colored
noise with integrated autocorrelation time $\tau_c$, showing that
$\tau_c$ enters as a multiplicative factor on the bound without
changing its functional form.
\S\ref{app:theory_lambda_scope} states precisely why the
$\lambda^2$ variance scaling is a local rather than a global
prediction, and documents the measured non-monotonicity of the
3-point $\lambda$ sweep.
\S\ref{app:theory_jpi} unpacks what $J_\pi$ represents in a VABL
architecture: the encoder-subspace column through
attention/GRU/$\phi_\theta$ by which teammate one-hot inputs
influence aux-head predictions, and why each fix path (mean
pooling, stop-gradient, critic-side placement) drives this
projection toward zero.
\S\ref{app:theory_scope} collects the six scope limits and open
questions at the end of the main-body theory section in fully
expanded form, including the SMAX residual mean gap, the
critic-side reversal, and the proxy-plot categorical structure.
\S\ref{app:theory_related_formalisms} places our theory objects
alongside five recent gradient-interference and non-stationarity
formalisms (COALA-PG, DG-PG, ROCKET, GAC, Trust-Region
Decomposition) and states precisely which formal assumptions our
setting violates relative to each.

\subsection{Linearization, locality, and the analysis window}
\label{app:theory_linearization}

The first-order expansion $\varepsilon(t) \approx J_\pi (\pi_j^{(t)}
- \bar\pi_j)$ is exact when the target-generating policy stays
within a small neighborhood of the time-averaged policy $\bar\pi_j$
over the analysis window. Formally, write $g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t) =
g_{\mathrm{aux}}(\bar\pi_j) + J_\pi \Delta_t +
\mathcal{O}(\|\Delta_t\|^2)$ with $\Delta_t = \pi_j^{(t)} -
\bar\pi_j$; the drop-to-noise factorization
$\Sigma_\varepsilon \approx J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top$ is
accurate to $\mathcal{O}(\|\Delta\|^3)$ in the window's maximal
drift. Restricting to the final $50\%$ of training is important
because policy convergence implies smaller per-iteration drift, so
the linearization is tightest precisely where we measure the
cosine-std proxy. Mid-training, where the worst cross-seed
instability actually occurs, the linearization is less tight, so
the theory's quantitative predictions are most useful for
late-training equilibrium behavior and qualitative for mid-training
transients.

\subsection{Colored-noise correction to Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}}
\label{app:theory_colored}

Proposition~\ref{prop:variance} treats $\varepsilon(t)$ as i.i.d.\
to obtain a clean closed-form bound. Real $\varepsilon(t)$ is
autocorrelated because consecutive target-generating policies
differ by a single gradient step, so $\pi_j^{(t+1)}$ and
$\pi_j^{(t)}$ are close in parameter space. Let $\tau_c = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}
\rho_\varepsilon(k)$ denote the integrated autocorrelation time of
$\varepsilon$, where $\rho_\varepsilon(k) = \mathrm{Cov}[\varepsilon(t),
\varepsilon(t+k)] / \mathrm{Var}[\varepsilon(t)]$. For an AR(1)
approximation with decay rate $\phi \in [0,1)$, $\tau_c =
(1+\phi)/(1-\phi)$. The steady-state bound becomes
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\|\delta\|^2]_{\mathrm{colored}}
    \;\propto\;
    \tau_c \cdot
    \frac{\alpha \,\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \,\Sigma_\pi\, J_\pi^\top)}
         {\lambda_{\min}(H)}.
    \label{eq:colored_bound}
\end{equation}
The dependence on $\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top)$ and
$\lambda_{\min}(H)$ is unchanged; $\tau_c$ enters as a
multiplicative factor that can be large for fast-learning teammates
(AR(1) with $\phi$ close to $1$). This means the i.i.d.\ bound may
underestimate absolute variance substantially, but the
\emph{ordering} across conditions is preserved under any uniform
$\tau_c$.

\subsection{Scope of the $\lambda$ prediction}
\label{app:theory_lambda_scope}

Since $g_{\mathrm{aux}} \propto \lambda$ by construction, the
first-order bound predicts displacement variance $\propto \lambda^2$.
Our 3-point $\lambda$ sweep (Appendix~\ref{app:lambda}) is
\emph{not} monotonic: Final50 std at $\lambda \in \{0.001, 0.01,
0.05\}$ is $\{6.37, 1.67, 8.55\}$ respectively. The bound holds
locally (variance increases from $\lambda = 0.01$ to $\lambda =
0.05$) but fails across the full range because aux-head
representations co-adapt with $\lambda$: at very small $\lambda$
the aux head receives too little signal to organize its
representation, so $J_\pi$ itself becomes a function of $\lambda$
in a way the first-order analysis does not capture. The prediction
should be read as ``within a $\lambda$ range where the aux-head
representation is qualitatively unchanged,'' not as a global claim.

\subsection{Interpreting $J_\pi$ in VABL}
\label{app:theory_jpi}

$J_\pi$ is the sensitivity of the aux gradient to the
target-policy action distribution, evaluated around $\bar\pi_j$. In
VABL with teammate-action targets, its column space coincides with
the pathway through attention/GRU/$\phi_\theta$ by which teammate
one-hot inputs influence aux-head predictions. It is large when the
aux head is tightly tuned to the current target, and small when
the encoder is structurally insensitive: mean pooling collapses
heterogeneous target sources onto a single mean vector, so
different target identities produce identical contexts;
stop-gradient on $b_t^i$ cuts the encoder-side coupling while
leaving the aux MLP trainable; critic-side placement routes
$g_{\mathrm{aux}}$ through the critic parameters entirely, so the
actor encoder never receives the aux signal. All three interventions drive the \emph{encoder-subspace}
projection of $J_\pi$ toward zero without eliminating the aux loss
itself.

\subsection{Open questions and scope limits}
\label{app:theory_scope}

The main text compresses six caveats to the first-order model;
this section states each in full.
\textbf{(i) Autocorrelation.} The i.i.d.\ noise assumption gives
the variance-scaling \emph{shape} but not absolute magnitude;
colored-noise correction (Appendix~\ref{app:theory_colored})
multiplies the bound by $\tau_c$.
\textbf{(ii) Local-quadratic approximation.} The bound is tightest
in late training where drift is smallest, which is also where the
pathology is least severe; it applies less tightly mid-training
where the worst cross-seed instability actually occurs.
\textbf{(iii) Proxy--variance ordering.} The $R^2 = 0.94$ fit
(Figure~\ref{fig:proxy}) is dominated by the categorical separation
between Full (non-zero proxy) and the three pathway-zero
conditions. The plot verifies ordering more than slope.
\textbf{(iv) Critic-side reversal.} The theory explains how
severing $J_\pi$ in the encoder subspace eliminates encoder-side
directional interference but says nothing about what happens when
$g_{\mathrm{aux}}$ is routed through the critic. The critic-side
reversal we observe ($d = -0.79$, Appendix~\ref{app:arch_sweep})
falls outside the scope of Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}.
\textbf{(v) SMAX residual mean gap.} Fix paths recover variance
but not mean on SMAX; this is inconsistent with a purely zero-mean
directional-noise mechanism (symmetric oscillation should not bias
the mean). Either a secondary pathology coexists in SMAX or the
zero-mean assumption of $\varepsilon(t)$ is too strong there.
\textbf{(vi) $\lambda$ non-monotonicity.} The monotonic-$\lambda$
prediction fails globally because aux-head representations co-adapt
with $\lambda$, making $J_\pi$ itself $\lambda$-dependent
(Appendix~\ref{app:theory_lambda_scope}).

\subsection{Relation to other gradient-interference formalisms}
\label{app:theory_related_formalisms}

Our theory sits at a specific axis within a broader literature of
gradient-interference and non-stationarity diagnostics; related
formal objects are listed below.

\textbf{DG-PG exogeneity assumption~\cite{yang2026dgpg}.} Their
framework treats a guidance term $G$ with reference trajectory
$\tilde{x}_t$ satisfying $\nabla_\theta \tilde{x}_t = 0$
(Assumption 3.1) as a variance-reduction signal for cooperative
MARL. Their Theorem 4.2 then gives the closed-form optimal
combining weight
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\alpha \in (0,1)} \mathrm{Var}(\hat{g}^i_{\mathrm{DG}})
    = \frac{\sigma_J^2 \sigma_G^2 (1 - \rho^2)}
           {\sigma_J^2 + \sigma_G^2 + 2\rho\,\sigma_J\sigma_G},
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_J^2, \sigma_G^2$ are the variances of the
policy-gradient and guidance-gradient estimators and $\rho$ is
their correlation. In our setting the aux target is generated by a
co-evolving policy (the teammate's in Full VABL), so
$\nabla_\theta \tilde{x}_t \neq 0$. Assumption~3.1 is violated,
which is precisely why $\Sigma_\varepsilon$ is driven by
$\Sigma_\pi$ rather than collapsing. Reading $\hat{g}_J = g_\pi$,
$\hat{g}_G = g_{\mathrm{aux}}$, $\rho = \mathbb{E}_t[\cos]$, the
DG-PG formula prescribes an optimal $\alpha^*(t)$ that our
drift-gated aux controller approximates by binary gating.

\textbf{COALA-PG batched meta-POMDP~\cite{meulemans2024coala}.}
Meulemans et al.\ formalize cooperative-MARL learning as a
batched co-player shaping POMDP whose meta-state is the joint
parameter vector $(\phi^i, \phi^{-i})$. The distribution over
meta-trajectories induced by the meta-dynamics is the rigorous
object that our $\Sigma_\pi$ informally approximates.

\textbf{Trust-Region Decomposition~\cite{li2022trustregion}.} Li
et al.\ introduce $\delta$-stationarity, an explicit
non-stationarity measure bounded by the KL-divergence of
consecutive joint policies. When we say ``target-policy drift,''
this is the formal quantity being measured; the KL bound gives an
upper estimate on $\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\pi)$ in natural parameter
coordinates.

\textbf{ROCKET cross-layer interference~\cite{sun2026rocket}.} Sun
et al.\ analyze gradient interference in multi-layer representation
alignment, decomposing the squared gradient norm as
$\|G\|^2 = \sum_l \alpha_l^2 \|v_l\|^2 +
\sum_{a \neq b} \alpha_a \alpha_b \langle v_a, v_b \rangle$, where
$\langle v_a, v_b \rangle = g_a^\top M_{ab} g_b$ is a bilinear
form on teacher-error signals. Their cross-term is the
static (cross-layer) analog of our temporal cross-term: both
identify $\langle g_a, g_b \rangle$ structure as the source of
conflict, and both propose structural constraints (shared projector
for ROCKET, stop-gradient for us) that eliminate the conflict
without weight tuning.

\textbf{GAC consecutive-step cosine~\cite{xu2026gac}.} Xu et al.\
diagnose asynchronous LLM RL instability via the consecutive-step
cosine $c_t = \langle g_t, g_{t-1} \rangle / (\|g_t\|\|g_{t-1}\|)$
of a single gradient stream. This is a temporal
autocorrelation-at-lag-1; our $\cos(g_\pi, g_{\mathrm{aux}})$ is a
cross-correlation at lag-0. Both are cosine-based first-order
diagnostics and share the ``late-window temporal std'' estimation
pattern, but diagnose different instabilities: theirs is staleness
across async workers, ours is structured-target interference within
a single worker.

\subsection{Target-source distinguishing test: full specification}
\label{app:method_targets}

The test compares Full VABL with three alternative aux-target
sources, holding aux network, capacity, and the aux-to-encoder
gradient pathway identical across conditions:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Teammate co-learning (Full).} Standard VABL: the
aux target for step $t+1$ is the actual teammate action
$a_{t+1}^j$. Targets are both \emph{structured} (come from a real
policy) and \emph{non-stationary} (drift with teammate co-learning).
\item \textbf{Frozen-policy targets.} At iteration~0 we snapshot
the initial (random-init) agent parameters as $\theta_0$ and
freeze them. At each subsequent iteration, the aux target for step
$t+1$ is $\arg\max_a \pi_{\theta_0}(a \mid o_{t+1}^j)$ applied to
the observed rollout state, rather than the teammate's actual
action. Targets are \emph{stationary} (no drift with teammate
co-learning) but remain \emph{structured} (from a real policy).
Realizes the $\Sigma_\pi \to 0$ limit.
\item \textbf{Uniform random targets.} The aux target for step
$t+1$ is an integer drawn uniformly at random from
$\{0, \ldots, |\mathcal{A}| - 1\}$, resampled each iteration.
Targets are \emph{stationary in expectation} and
\emph{unstructured} (no co-learning signal, no semantic coupling
to teammates). Realizes the $J_\pi \to 0$ limit.
\end{itemize}

All three interventions leave aux capacity, architecture, gradient
pathway, and loss form identical to Full; they vary \emph{only}
the source and structure of the aux target. The
capacity-consumption hypothesis predicts all three conditions to
be pathological. The directional-interference hypothesis predicts
a graded recovery: teammate-co-learning stays pathological, while
frozen and random targets both move back toward the No Aux regime
(at $n{=}5$ the frozen- and random-vs-No-Aux Cohen's $d$ bootstrap
CIs both cross zero; only the teammate-vs-No-Aux comparison
resolves).

We compare against four methods spanning three paradigms:

\section{Full Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}}
\label{app:proof}

Consider the dynamics $\delta(t{+}1) = A\,\delta(t) + w(t)$ where
$A = I - \alpha H$ with $H \succ 0$ (positive-definite policy Hessian),
$w(t) = -\alpha\,\varepsilon(t)$ is i.i.d.\ with $\mathbb{E}[w] = 0$
and $\mathrm{Cov}[w] = \alpha^2 \Sigma_\varepsilon$.

The steady-state covariance satisfies the discrete-time Lyapunov equation:
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma_\delta = A\,\Sigma_\delta\,A^T + \alpha^2\,\Sigma_\varepsilon.
\end{equation}

Since $H$ is symmetric, let $H = Q \Lambda Q^T$ with eigenvalues
$h_1 \leq h_2 \leq \cdots \leq h_d$. In the eigenbasis, $A$ is diagonal
with entries $a_i = 1 - \alpha h_i$, and the Lyapunov equation decouples:
\begin{equation}
    (\Sigma_\delta)_{ii} = \frac{\alpha^2\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}
                                 {1 - a_i^2}
    = \frac{\alpha^2\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}
           {2\alpha h_i - \alpha^2 h_i^2}.
\end{equation}

For standard learning rates $\alpha h_i \ll 1$, the denominator is
approximately $2\alpha h_i$, giving:
\begin{equation}
    (\Sigma_\delta)_{ii} \approx
    \frac{\alpha\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}{2 h_i}.
\end{equation}

The trace (total steady-state variance) is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\|\delta\|^2] = \mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\delta)
    \approx \frac{\alpha}{2} \sum_i
    \frac{(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}{h_i}
    \;\leq\; \frac{\alpha\,\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\varepsilon)}
                   {2\,h_{\min}},
\end{equation}
which establishes the bound in Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}. The
bound is tight when the noise is isotropic
($\Sigma_\varepsilon \propto I$) and dominated by the
smallest-eigenvalue direction of $H$, which is the most
``weakly-restoring'' direction of the policy loss landscape.

\section{Proxy--Variance Plot}
\label{app:proxy}

This appendix supports the ``From cosine to proxy'' paragraph in
\S\ref{sec:theory}. Recall that linearizing $\cos(g_\pi,
g_{\mathrm{aux}})$ at fixed norms gives
$\mathrm{Var}_t[\cos] \approx \hat g_\pi^\top \Sigma_\varepsilon
\hat g_\pi / \|\bar g_{\mathrm{aux}}\|^2$, so the cosine temporal
standard deviation is a finite-sample proxy for normalized
$\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top)^{1/2}$. If the theory is
correct, this proxy should predict the Final50 cross-seed standard
deviation ordering.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{figures/neurips/f_proxy_variance.pdf}
\caption{Cosine temporal std (proxy for $\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top)$ normalized by gradient magnitudes) vs Final50 cross-seed std across four logged conditions on Overcooked AA. Three zero-proxy conditions reach zero by pathway construction; Full is the only condition with non-zero proxy and has elevated variance. Linear $R^2 = 0.94$ across conditions; the ordering is the main prediction, not the slope.}
\label{fig:proxy}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{How to read the plot.}
Each point is one of the four logged conditions from ExpB (Full,
No Aux, Stop-grad, Anneal), aggregated over 5 seeds. The $x$-axis
is the cosine temporal standard deviation measured in the late
$50\%$ training window; the $y$-axis is the Final50 cross-seed
standard deviation. Three of the four conditions have proxy
\emph{exactly zero} by construction: No~Aux has no aux gradient so
$g_{\mathrm{aux}} = 0$; Stop-grad severs the encoder-subspace
projection of $J_\pi$; Anneal drives $\lambda \to 0$ in the late
window. Only Full retains a non-zero proxy, and it is the only
condition with elevated Final50 variance.

\paragraph{What the fit verifies.}
The $R^2 = 0.94$ linear fit confirms the ordering prediction: the
condition with non-zero proxy ($J_\pi \cdot \Sigma_\pi$ both
non-zero) has the highest cross-seed variance, and the three
conditions with zero proxy cluster together at low variance. Since
three of four $x$-values are identically zero, the fit is
dominated by the categorical separation rather than a continuous
slope. Within Full's 5 seeds, per-seed cosine std correlates with
per-seed peak-to-Final50 drop at $r = +0.40$, in the predicted
direction but weak at $n = 5$.

\paragraph{Scope.}
The proxy is a first-order approximation and shares the scope
limits of Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}
(Appendix~\ref{app:theory_scope}). In particular, the
normalization by $\|\bar g_{\mathrm{aux}}\|^2$ means the proxy
measures the \emph{direction} of the aux gradient relative to the
policy gradient, not the aux gradient's absolute energy; a condition
with a large-magnitude but direction-stable aux gradient would read
as low proxy without necessarily being free of interference.

or mean pooling) independently while keeping all other hyperparameters fixed.
Each configuration is run with and without the auxiliary loss (5 seeds
each, 60 runs total). A MAAC-style variant places the auxiliary head on the
centralized critic instead of the actor.

\vspace{2pt}
{\footnotesize $^\ddagger$Both conditions exhibit high intrinsic cross-seed variance ($\sigma > 145$) in both the +aux and $-$aux cells, making the aux effect undetectable.}

The pathology appears whenever \emph{any} recurrence (GRU or LSTM) is paired
with \emph{any} learned attention mechanism (cross-attention or additive) and
a constant auxiliary loss on the actor. Mean pooling, which has no learned
attention parameters, is immune ($d = -0.30$). The MAAC-style
critic-auxiliary configuration reverses the effect ($d = -0.79$): the
auxiliary gradient flows through the critic rather than the actor's belief
encoder, so it corrupts value estimates (which are recomputed each epoch)
rather than the policy representation (which accumulates damage through
recurrence).


\section{Environments, Baselines, and Metrics}
\label{app:env_baselines_metrics}

This appendix expands \S\ref{sec:method}'s parenthetical summary of
the experimental infrastructure.

\subsection{Environments}
\label{app:env_specs}

We evaluate four MARL environments via
JaxMARL~\cite{rutherford2024jaxmarl} and one supervised control.

\textbf{Overcooked}~\cite{carroll2019utility}: \emph{Asymmetric
Advantages} and \emph{Cramped Room}, 2 agents, horizon 400,
25{,}024 episodes ($\approx$10M steps), 64 vectorized envs.

\textbf{Hanabi 2-player}~\cite{bard2020hanabi}: turn-based
partial-information card game, horizon 150 (re-run from an initial
$h{=}80$ pilot that truncated games), 25K episodes.

\textbf{MPE simple\_spread}~\cite{lowe2017multi}: 3 symmetric
agents, horizon 25, 100K episodes.

\textbf{SMAX 3v3}~\cite{rutherford2024jaxmarl}: 3 allies versus 3
heuristic enemies, horizon 100, 50K episodes, covering both
SMAC~\cite{samvelyan2019starcraft} and SMAC
v2~\cite{ellis2023smacv2} scenarios (tactical combat).

\textbf{CIFAR-100}~\cite{krizhevsky2009learning} (supervised
control): small Vision Transformer~\cite{dosovitskiy2020image}
(4 layers, 192 dim, 4 heads, patch size 4) with a RotNet auxiliary
head~\cite{gidaris2018unsupervised} (4-way rotation prediction),
$\lambda = 0.05$ constant, 200 epochs, AdamW with cosine schedule.

\subsection{Baselines}
\label{app:baselines_full}

We compare against four methods spanning three paradigms.

\textbf{MAPPO}~\cite{yu2022surprising}: decentralized policy
gradient with centralized critic; the standard cooperative-MARL
baseline.

\textbf{TarMAC}~\cite{das2019tarmac}: targeted attention-based
inter-agent communication.

\textbf{CommNet}~\cite{sukhbaatar2016commnet}: broadcast
communication via hidden-state averaging.

\textbf{AERIAL}~\cite{phan2023attention}: attention over teammate
hidden states; our port uses PPO for comparability with the rest of
the matrix, whereas the original is QMIX-based.

All baselines are reimplemented in JAX~\cite{bradbury2018jax} using
JaxMARL~\cite{rutherford2024jaxmarl} with matched hyperparameters,
env wrapper, and evaluation protocol, so cross-method differences
reflect algorithmic choices rather than infrastructure drift. None
of these baselines instantiates the attention + teammate-prediction-aux
design pattern; they therefore support the training-budget-artifact
claim (\S\ref{sec:budget}) but do not serve as controls for the
core mechanism. Direct re-implementation of Dynamic
Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic} or BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal}, which
do instantiate the pattern, is left to future work; we rely on
Zhai et al.'s independently-reported Static-Belief failure mode as
external corroboration (\S\ref{sec:related}).

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{app:metrics_full}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Best}: maximum episode reward (or maximum test
    accuracy in the supervised control) achieved during training.
    \item \textbf{Final50} (MARL) / \textbf{Final5} (vision): mean
    of the last 50 episodes (or last 5 epochs), capturing the
    policy's stable convergence level.
    \item \textbf{Collapse \%}:
    $({\mathrm{Best} - \mathrm{Final}}) / {\mathrm{Best}} \times 100$,
    used only when discussing the broader literature's
    coordination-collapse framing.
    \item \textbf{Cross-seed std}: population standard deviation
    across the 5 random seeds per cell; the headline stability
    metric for our pathology.
    \item \textbf{Cohen's $d$}~\cite{cohen1988statistical} with
    bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals ($n_{\mathrm{boot}} =
    10{,}000$) for effect-size comparisons. A CI that crosses zero
    signals a non-resolvable effect at $n=5$.
\end{itemize}

All MARL numbers report mean $\pm$ std over 5 random seeds unless
otherwise noted.

\section{Figure and Reader Accessibility}
\label{app:accessibility}

\paragraph{Reader accessibility.}
The paper is structured for a reviewer scan: the title
opens with a curiosity prompt before stating the mechanism; the
abstract puts problem, mechanism, and what-the-failure-is-not in
three citation-free sentences; an italicized one-line principle
closes the first intro paragraph; Figure~\ref{fig:causal_chain}
gives a four-box conceptual chain (target-policy updates $\to
\Sigma_\pi \to \Sigma_\varepsilon = J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top \to$
instability) before any math;
contributions are a scannable bulleted list with noun-phrase labels;
and the Theory section opens with a plain-language intuition
sentence before the four-step outline. Numeric thresholds and
bootstrap CIs are deferred from the introduction to Methodology and
Results, where they carry their full meaning.

\paragraph{Figure accessibility.}
All figures in this paper are designed for accessibility across viewing
conditions. One author has color vision deficiency, which motivated a
systematic approach to figure design:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Color palette.} We use the IBM colorblind-safe palette
    (magenta, blue, purple, orange, gold, teal), which maintains perceptual
    distinctness under deuteranopia, protanopia, and tritanopia. The
    pathological configuration (Full VABL) uses magenta throughout all
    figures for consistent identification.

    \item \textbf{Redundant encoding.} Every data series is distinguished by
    at least two independent visual channels. Dot plots use distinct marker
    shapes (cross, square, diamond, circle). Bar charts use hatching patterns
    (diagonal, dots, backslash, crosshatch) in addition to color. Line plots
    use distinct line styles (solid, dashed, dash-dot, dotted) with large
    markers.

    \item \textbf{Grayscale readability.} All figures remain interpretable
    when printed in black-and-white. We verified this by converting each
    figure to grayscale and confirming that all data series remain
    distinguishable through shape, pattern, and line style alone.

    \item \textbf{Font consistency.} Figure text (axis labels, legends,
    annotations) uses the same serif font family as the paper body to
    maintain visual coherence. We avoid matplotlib's default sans-serif
    rendering.

    \item \textbf{No duplicate titles.} Figures use LaTeX captions only,
    with no redundant matplotlib titles baked into the image. This avoids
    visual clutter and ensures consistent typography.
\end{itemize}

We encourage the MARL community to adopt similar accessibility practices.
Approximately 8\% of males and 0.5\% of females have some form of color
vision deficiency, and many readers access papers in printed grayscale.
Relying solely on color to distinguish data series excludes these readers.
