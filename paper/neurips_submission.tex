\begin{abstract}
Auxiliary prediction losses are used widely to stabilize representation
learning under sparse rewards, from UNREAL~\cite{jaderberg2016reinforcement}
and world models~\cite{hafner2023mastering} to self-predictive
representations~\cite{schwarzer2021dataefficient} and cooperative MARL
methods such as Dynamic Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic} and
BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal}. We argue that a previously unstudied
learning-dynamics principle governs when this pattern succeeds and
when it fails: \emph{auxiliary gradients carrying targets that are
both structured (task-coupled) and non-stationary (drifting as other
learners update) inject directional noise into the shared encoder
that dominates policy gradients near convergence}. The failure is
\emph{directional}, not magnitude-based, and \emph{target-driven},
not multi-task conflict. We test this principle in cooperative MARL
where co-learning teammates provide the natural non-stationarity.
A target-source distinguishing experiment holds aux capacity,
architecture, and the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway fixed and
varies only the target: co-learning teammate targets are pathological
($463 \pm 9$); frozen-initial-policy targets (structured but stationary)
partially recover ($470 \pm 5$); uniform random targets (unstructured,
stationary) fully recover the no-aux regime ($474 \pm 2$). A
complementary $8\times$ aux-capacity sweep has no effect, ruling out
capacity consumption. Supervised CIFAR-100 (stationary targets by
construction) defines the principle's boundary: zero effect, as the
theory predicts. The target-source ordering itself is the principle's prediction:
random targets (stationary, unstructured) should show no effect,
frozen targets (stationary, structured) should reduce the effect,
teammate targets (non-stationary, structured) should be fully
pathological. The experiments confirm this ordering.
Effect size scales with coordination complexity across four MARL
benchmarks (Hanabi, Cramped Room, AA, SMAX) and with environment
teammate-policy drift rate, as the principle predicts. Three
gradient-pathway interventions recover the no-aux regime on
Overcooked. All 375{+} runs and code at
\url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/aux-loss-considered-harmful}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Auxiliary prediction losses are ubiquitous in modern deep learning:
UNREAL~\cite{jaderberg2016reinforcement}, world
models~\cite{hafner2023mastering}, self-predictive
representations~\cite{schwarzer2021dataefficient}, and the MARL
belief-learning pattern of Dynamic Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic} and
BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal} all attach a constant-weight auxiliary
loss to a shared encoder. Most of the time it helps. Sometimes it
silently corrupts late-training convergence with cross-seed variance
that looks like ordinary RL noise. This paper identifies the
learning-dynamics principle that separates the two regimes.

\textbf{The principle.} Auxiliary gradients carrying targets that are
both \emph{structured} (coupled to the current task state) and
\emph{non-stationary} (drifting as other learners update) inject
\emph{directional} noise into the shared encoder that dominates policy
gradients near convergence. The failure is not magnitude-based (aux-to-policy
magnitude ratios stay below $0.25$; PCGrad~\cite{yu2020gradient},
GradNorm~\cite{chen2018gradnorm}-style gradient surgery does not
address it), not multi-task conflict (a single auxiliary is enough),
and not capacity-driven ($8\times$ aux capacity sweep has no effect).
What matters is the \emph{target}: when it drifts with another
learner's updates, the gradient direction becomes a non-stationary
process that shrinking policy gradients near convergence cannot
dominate. Table~\ref{tab:contrast} locates this claim relative to
prior work.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\small
\caption{The principle we identify versus adjacent prior work.}
\label{tab:contrast}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule

\midrule
Mechanism & magnitude interference & \textbf{directional interference} \\
Targets & static / stationary & \textbf{non-stationary} \\
Setting & multi-task conflict & \textbf{single-task + auxiliary} \\
Driver & gradient norm imbalance & \textbf{target drift across learners} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Cooperative MARL as test bed.} Co-learning teammates are a
clean natural source of structured non-stationary targets: a
teammate-action-prediction aux target is coupled to the game state
\emph{and} drifts as teammates update. Zhai et
al.~\cite{zhai2023dynamic}'s Static-Belief ablation reports exactly
this pattern (Traffic Junction success $73.4\% \to 71.9\%$); we
characterize the mechanism behind their observation. We construct
\textbf{VABL} (\S\ref{sec:background}) as a minimal architecture in
this class and test the principle with three experiments.
\emph{(i) Target-source distinguishing test} (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}):
the principle predicts the ordering random $<$ frozen $<$ teammate in
pathology severity; the data confirm it. \emph{(ii) Aux-capacity
sweep}: $8\times$ variation has no effect. \emph{(iii) Boundary}:
pathology scales with coordination-induced teammate drift across four
MARL benchmarks; MPE's slow symmetric drift and supervised CIFAR-100
show zero effect as the principle predicts for stationary targets.

\textbf{Contributions.} \textbf{(1)}~A learning-dynamics principle:
structured non-stationary auxiliary targets inject directional noise
that dominates policy gradients near convergence; the principle makes
a testable ordering prediction over target sources.
\textbf{(2)}~Causal identification in cooperative MARL via a
target-source test and an $8\times$ aux-capacity sweep that jointly
rule out generic capacity-consumption and multi-task-conflict
alternatives. \textbf{(3)}~Boundary of the principle: stationary
supervised targets show zero effect (CIFAR-100) and slow symmetric
drift shows near-zero effect (MPE), while coordination-induced
teammate drift produces pathology on four MARL benchmarks.
\textbf{(4)}~Three gradient-pathway interventions ($\lambda$-annealing,
stop-gradient on the belief, critic-side aux placement) recover the
no-aux regime on Overcooked; SMAX retains a residual mean gap with
non-monotonic $\lambda$ response that we flag as open.
\textbf{(5)}~A reproducible 375{+}-run benchmark with 5 seeds and
bootstrap CIs. As a secondary finding (\S\ref{sec:budget}), much of
the widely reported Overcooked coordination collapse is a
training-budget artifact.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Auxiliary losses in RL.}
UNREAL~\cite{jaderberg2016reinforcement} pioneered auxiliary tasks;
subsequent work uses world-model
losses~\cite{hafner2023mastering}, self-predictive
representations~\cite{schwarzer2021dataefficient,fang2024predictive},
and contrastive objectives~\cite{laskin2020curl}. Theoretical analyses
of auxiliary-task
dynamics~\cite{lyle2021effect,voelcker2024selfprediction} cover TD and
latent self-prediction; \cite{du2018adapting,lin2019adaptive} argue
constant weights are suboptimal.
\cite{moalla2024norepnotrust} document on-policy PPO representation
collapse under non-stationarity; \cite{chua2024successor} show
successor-feature losses collapse basis features to cosine-to-1,
resolved by stop-gradient in the reward loss (an analogue to our
stop-gradient fix path). In MARL,
XP-MARL~\cite{xu2024xpmarl} prioritizes training samples for
non-stationarity; BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal} and Dynamic
Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic} directly instantiate the design pattern
we study (attention-based belief encoder $+$ fixed-weight
teammate-future-prediction aux). Zhai et al.~\cite{zhai2023dynamic}'s
Static-Belief ablation (their Table~2) reports that naive co-training
drops Traffic Junction success from $73.4\%$ to $71.9\%$; their fix
is a meta-learned dynamic context.

\paragraph{Attention and stop-gradient.}
AERIAL~\cite{phan2023attention} uses QMIX-based attention over
teammate hidden states (no auxiliary head);
MAAC~\cite{iqbal2019actor} places attention in the centralized critic;
MAT~\cite{wen2022mat} uses an encoder-decoder for centralized policy
learning. None pair attention-based \emph{decentralized} belief
encoding with a constant-weight teammate-future-prediction aux loss.
In SSL, BYOL~\cite{grill2020bootstrap},
SimSiam~\cite{chen2021exploring}, and DINO~\cite{caron2021emerging} use
stop-gradient to prevent representation collapse; our stop-gradient
fix path is the MARL analogue.

\paragraph{Gradient interference and coordination collapse.}
PCGrad~\cite{yu2020gradient}, GradNorm~\cite{chen2018gradnorm},
HyperMARL~\cite{tessera2025hypermarl}, and multi-timescale
learning~\cite{nekoei2023dealing} project, reweight, or decouple
gradients by magnitude or across agents; direction-oriented
multi-objective
optimization~\cite{xiao2023sdmgrad,zhang2025mgda,patapati2025graphcoloring}
schedules by inter-task gradient conflict. Offline
MARL~\cite{tilbury2024coordination} identifies a distinct directional
pathology from data-mean-biased best responses. Our pathology is
intra-agent, directional rather than magnitude-driven
(Figure~\ref{fig:gradients}c), and environment-dependent.
Overcooked~\cite{carroll2019utility} is widely studied with prior
reports of severe degradation~\cite{yu2022surprising,rashid2020monotonic};
recent benchmarking shows algorithm rankings depend sharply on
training budget~\cite{formanek2024mirage,papadopoulos2025extended,bettini2024benchmarl}.
At 10M steps most reported ``collapse'' is insufficient training; the
residual instability we identify is distinct and gradient-mediated.

\section{Background: Attention-Based Belief Learning}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================================

The design pattern we investigate, attention-based belief encoders
trained with constant-weight auxiliary prediction losses targeting
teammate future behavior, has two recent published instances.
BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal} uses a shared LSTM with attention over
teammate messages and belief-decoder heads predicting teammates'
future motion and reward with fixed weights; Dynamic
Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic} uses soft attention over episode history
with a VAE-based head predicting teammates' future actions with fixed
weights. These sit within the broader family of world-model and
self-predictive architectures that use constant auxiliary
weights~\cite{hafner2023mastering,schwarzer2021dataefficient,jaderberg2016reinforcement}.
We construct \textbf{VABL} as a minimal architecture in this class,
operating on cooperative Dec-POMDPs~\cite{oliehoek2016concise}, and
chosen so that attention, the auxiliary loss, and the gradient flow
between them can be manipulated independently.

\paragraph{Belief update.}
Each agent $i$ maintains a recurrent latent belief $b_t^i \in
\mathbb{R}^{d_h}$, updated at each timestep from (i) an observation
embedding $\phi_\theta(o_t^i) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ and (ii) a social
context vector $c_t^i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ aggregated from observable
teammate actions:
\begin{equation}
    b_t^i = \mathrm{GRU}\left(
        [\phi_\theta(o_t^i) \| c_t^i],\; b_{t-1}^i
    \right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{GRU}$ denotes a gated recurrent unit~\cite{cho2014gru}.

\paragraph{Attention context.}
The social context is computed via multi-head attention~\cite{vaswani2017attention} over action embeddings
of visible teammates:
\begin{equation}
    c_t^i = \mathrm{MHA}\left(
        \mathrm{query} = W_q b_{t-1}^i, \quad
        \mathrm{keys/values} = \{\psi_\theta(a_{t-1}^j) + e_j\}_{j \neq i}
    \right),
\end{equation}
where $\psi_\theta$ is an action encoder, $e_j \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ is
a learnable identity embedding for teammate $j$, and
$m_t^{i \leftarrow j} \in \{0,1\}$ is a per-timestep visibility mask (in
Overcooked, whether agent $j$ is within agent $i$'s visible tile
region; in SMAX, within sight range; in MPE, within observation
radius). The attention softmax zeros out masked entries, so invisible
teammates contribute neither to $c_t^i$ nor to the auxiliary loss. When
no attention is used (the mean-pooling ablation), $c_t^i$ is the
unweighted average of visible teammate action embeddings.

\paragraph{Policy and auxiliary heads.}
The policy $\pi_\theta(a_t^i \mid b_t^i)$ is a linear head on the belief.
The auxiliary predictor $\hat{\pi}_\varphi(\cdot \mid b_t^i)$ is a 2-layer
MLP that predicts each visible teammate's next action from the current
belief, trained with cross-entropy:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}}^i = \mathbb{E}\left[
        \sum_{j \neq i} m_{t+1}^{i \leftarrow j}\,
        \mathrm{CE}(\hat{\pi}_\varphi(\cdot \mid b_t^i),\, a_{t+1}^j)
    \right].
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The total loss combines a PPO policy gradient~\cite{schulman2017ppo}, a centralized value loss, entropy
regularization, and the auxiliary loss:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PPO}} +
    c_v \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{value}} +
    c_e \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{entropy}} +
    \lambda \sum_i \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}}^i,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = 0.05$ is the auxiliary loss weight. In the standard
configuration, $\lambda$ is held constant throughout training.

\paragraph{Gradient pathway.} The auxiliary loss gradient flows
through the belief $b_t^i$ into the attention mechanism, GRU, and
observation encoder $\phi_\theta$. Section~\ref{sec:results} identifies
this pathway as the source of the late-training instability.

\section{Theory: From Teammate Drift to Directional Interference}
\label{sec:theory}
% ============================================================================

We close the loop from co-learning to parameter variance in four
steps: (i) define directional interference and distinguish it from
magnitude interference; (ii) derive the aux-gradient noise covariance
from teammate-policy drift; (iii) bound late-training parameter
variance in terms of that covariance; (iv) state a threshold
condition for instability onset. The model is a \emph{first-order
mechanistic} derivation: it connects co-learning dynamics to observed
instability through causally upstream quantities ($\Sigma_\pi$,
$J_\pi$) rather than treating gradient noise as exogenous, using
standard linearization steps we make explicit below.

\paragraph{Step 1: defining directional interference.} Let
$g_\pi(t) = \nabla_\theta L_{\mathrm{PPO}}(\theta_t)$ and
$g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t) = \lambda \nabla_\theta L_{\mathrm{aux}}(\theta_t;
\pi_j^{(t)})$ be the two gradient signals the actor receives at
iteration $t$. Prior gradient-conflict work
(PCGrad~\cite{yu2020gradient}, GradNorm~\cite{chen2018gradnorm})
treats conflict as a \emph{magnitude} imbalance: persistent
$\mathbb{E}[g_\pi^\top g_{\mathrm{aux}}] < 0$ or
$\|g_{\mathrm{aux}}\| \gg \|g_\pi\|$. We define \emph{directional
interference} as temporal variance in gradient alignment:
\begin{equation}
    I_{\mathrm{dir}}
    = \mathrm{Var}_t\!\left[\tfrac{g_\pi(t)^\top g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t)}{\|g_\pi(t)\|\|g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t)\|}\right],
    \quad
    I_{\mathrm{mag}}
    = \left(\mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\tfrac{g_\pi(t)^\top g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t)}{\|g_\pi(t)\|\|g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t)\|}\right]\right)^2 .
    \label{eq:idir}
\end{equation}
High $I_{\mathrm{mag}}$ signals a persistent conflicting direction
(what PCGrad corrects). High $I_{\mathrm{dir}}$ with $I_{\mathrm{mag}}
\approx 0$ signals alignment that \emph{oscillates}: neither helpful
nor persistently harmful but corrosive to convergence.
Figure~\ref{fig:gradients} measures Full VABL's late-training signature
($\mathbb{E}[\cos] \approx 0$, $\mathrm{std}(\cos) = 0.185$), i.e.\
$I_{\mathrm{dir}} \gg I_{\mathrm{mag}}$.

\paragraph{Step 2: deriving $\Sigma_\varepsilon$ from teammate drift.}
Decompose $g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t) = \bar g_{\mathrm{aux}} + \varepsilon(t)$
into mean and zero-mean fluctuation. Because the aux loss depends on
teammate policy $\pi_j^{(t)}$, a first-order expansion around the
time-averaged policy $\bar\pi_j$ gives
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon(t) \approx J_\pi \,(\pi_j^{(t)} - \bar\pi_j),
    \qquad
    \Sigma_\varepsilon \approx J_\pi \,\Sigma_\pi\, J_\pi^\top,
    \qquad
    \Sigma_\pi = \mathrm{Cov}_t[\pi_j^{(t)}],
    \label{eq:drift_to_noise}
\end{equation}
where $J_\pi = \partial g_{\mathrm{aux}}/\partial \pi_j$ evaluated at
$\bar\pi_j$. The gradient noise covariance factors through the
teammate-policy drift covariance. Two limits:
frozen teammate ($\Sigma_\pi = 0$) gives $\Sigma_\varepsilon \to 0$;
a target constructed not to depend on teammate policy ($J_\pi = 0$,
e.g.\ random targets) also gives $\Sigma_\varepsilon \to 0$. The
target-source distinguishing test (\S\ref{sec:mechanism}) realizes
both limits empirically.

\paragraph{Step 3: noise-driven parameter variance.}
Near a local optimum $\theta^*$ the policy loss is approximately
quadratic with Hessian $H \succ 0$, giving $\nabla_\theta
L_{\mathrm{PPO}} = H(\theta - \theta^*)$ which shrinks as $\theta \to
\theta^*$. Centering around $\theta^*_{\mathrm{eff}} = \theta^* -
H^{-1} \bar g_{\mathrm{aux}}$, the displacement $\delta(t) = \theta(t)
- \theta^*_{\mathrm{eff}}$ evolves as
\begin{equation}
    \delta(t{+}1) = (I - \alpha H)\,\delta(t) - \alpha\,\varepsilon(t).
    \label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Drift-to-variance]
\label{prop:variance}
Under Eq.~\eqref{eq:dynamics} with $\varepsilon(t)$ treated as i.i.d.\
with covariance given by Eq.~\eqref{eq:drift_to_noise} and spectral
radius $\rho(I - \alpha H) < 1$, the steady-state displacement
variance satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\|\delta\|^2]
    \;\propto\;
    \frac{\alpha^2 \,\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \,\Sigma_\pi\, J_\pi^\top)}
         {\lambda_{\min}(H)}.
    \label{eq:variance_bound}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Step 4: threshold and testable predictions.}
Displacement competes with policy progress, so instability onset (in
the sense of elevated cross-seed Final50 variance) corresponds to
$\alpha^2 \mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \Sigma_\pi J_\pi^\top)/\lambda_{\min}(H)
\gtrsim C(\alpha, \eta)$, where $\eta$ is the per-iteration policy-
gradient norm and $C$ the policy-signal scale. This yields three
testable predictions. \textbf{(a) Stationarity null}: $\Sigma_\pi
\approx 0$ (supervised labels, symmetric slow drift) $\Rightarrow$
no pathology. \textbf{(b) Pathway null}: interventions that drive
$J_\pi \to 0$ in the encoder subspace (stop-gradient, critic-side
aux, annealed $\lambda$) $\Rightarrow$ recovery. \textbf{(c) Target
ordering}: ranking target sources by $\Sigma_\pi$ (random $= 0$,
frozen $= 0$, co-learning $> 0$) predicts ranking in pathology
severity. All three are tested in
\S\ref{sec:mechanism}--\ref{sec:cross_env}; the derivation (scope:
quadratic loss, linearized dynamics, i.i.d.\ noise, first-order
expansion) in Appendix~\ref{app:proof} uses the Lyapunov equation
$\Sigma_\delta = A \Sigma_\delta A^\top + \alpha^2 \Sigma_\varepsilon$
with $A = I - \alpha H$ and substitutes Eq.~\eqref{eq:drift_to_noise}.

\paragraph{Empirical proxy and derived algorithm.}
The cosine temporal std in Figure~\ref{fig:gradients} is a
finite-sample proxy for normalized $\mathrm{tr}(J_\pi \Sigma_\pi
J_\pi^\top)$. Across our four logged conditions the proxy predicts
the Final50 std ordering (Full: $0.18 \to 9.29$; No Aux / Stop-grad /
Anneal: $0 \to 4.40/4.24/2.87$); within-Full per-seed correlation
with peak-to-Final50 drop is $r=+0.40$ at $n{=}5$. The threshold
condition suggests a concrete adaptive controller, \emph{drift-gated
aux}: apply stop-gradient on the belief whenever a sliding-window
estimate of the cosine std exceeds a calibrated $\tau$. This
interpolates between the always-on and always-off limits we study
here; empirical evaluation is future work.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Ablation Matrix and Fix-Path Interventions}
We run VABL in a $2 \times 2$ ablation over attention vs.\ mean
pooling and auxiliary loss on ($\lambda = 0.05$ constant) vs.\ off,
yielding \textbf{Full} (attn+aux), \textbf{No Attention}, \textbf{No
Aux}, and \textbf{Neither}. Three gradient-only interventions on
Full: \textbf{Anneal} (linearly decay $\lambda$ from $0.05$ to $0$
over the first 50\% of training), \textbf{Stop-gradient}
(\texttt{stop\_gradient} on $b_t^i$ before the aux head; aux MLP
still trains but gradients do not reach the encoder), and
\textbf{Anneal + stop-gradient} (both combined).

\subsection{Target-Source Distinguishing Test}
\label{sec:method_distinguishing}

To distinguish a directional-interference mechanism driven by
structured, non-stationary aux targets from a generic
aux-capacity-consumption alternative, we run Full VABL with the aux
targets replaced by two alternative sources that preserve the aux
network, its capacity, and the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway
identically to Full:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Frozen-policy targets.} At iteration~0 we snapshot the
initial (random-init) agent parameters as $\theta_0$ and freeze them.
At each subsequent iteration, the aux target for step $t{+}1$ is the
argmax action of $\pi_{\theta_0}$ applied to the rollout observation,
rather than the actual teammate action. Targets are \emph{stationary}
(no drift with teammate co-learning) but remain \emph{structured}
(they come from a real policy).
\item \textbf{Uniform random targets.} The aux target for step $t{+}1$
is an integer drawn uniformly at random from $\{0,\dots,|\mathcal{A}|{-}1\}$,
resampled each iteration. Targets are \emph{stationary in expectation}
and \emph{unstructured} (no co-learning signal, no semantic coupling
to teammates).
\end{itemize}
Both interventions leave aux capacity, architecture, gradient
pathway, and loss form identical to Full; they vary \emph{only} the
source and structure of the target. The capacity-consumption
hypothesis predicts both to be pathological. The directional-interference
hypothesis predicts recovery to varying degrees, depending on whether
the residual structure is stationary (frozen) or absent (random).

\subsection{Environments}
We evaluate four MARL environments via
JaxMARL~\cite{rutherford2024jaxmarl} and one supervised control.
\textbf{Overcooked}~\cite{carroll2019utility}: \emph{Asymmetric
Advantages} and \emph{Cramped Room}, 2 agents, horizon 400,
25{,}024 episodes ($\approx$10M steps), 64 vectorized envs.
\textbf{Hanabi 2-player}~\cite{bard2020hanabi}: turn-based partial-info
card game, horizon 80, 25K episodes.
\textbf{MPE simple\_spread}~\cite{lowe2017multi}: 3 symmetric agents,
horizon 25, 100K episodes.
\textbf{SMAX 3v3}~\cite{rutherford2024jaxmarl}: 3 allies vs.\ 3
heuristic enemies, horizon 100, 50K episodes, covering both
SMAC~\cite{samvelyan2019starcraft} and SMAC
v2~\cite{ellis2023smacv2} scenarios (tactical combat).

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against four methods spanning three paradigms:
\textbf{MAPPO}~\cite{yu2022surprising} (decentralized policy gradient),
\textbf{TarMAC}~\cite{das2019tarmac} (targeted communication),
\textbf{CommNet}~\cite{sukhbaatar2016commnet} (broadcast communication),
and \textbf{AERIAL}~\cite{phan2023attention} (attention over teammate
hidden states; our port uses PPO for comparability, the original is
QMIX-based). All are reimplemented in JAX~\cite{bradbury2018jax} using
JaxMARL~\cite{rutherford2024jaxmarl} with matched hyperparameters,
env wrapper, and evaluation protocol. These baselines support the
training-budget-artifact claim (\S\ref{sec:budget}): none exhibits our
attention + teammate-prediction-aux design pattern, so they are not
controls for the core mechanism. Direct re-implementation of Dynamic
Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic} or BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal}, which do
instantiate the pattern, would be a stronger external comparison and
is left to future work; we rely instead on Zhai et al.'s
independently-reported Static-Belief failure mode as external
corroboration (\S\ref{sec:related}).

\subsection{Cross-Domain Test}

To test whether the pathology generalizes beyond MARL, we replicate the
$2 \times 2$ ablation on CIFAR-100~\cite{krizhevsky2009learning} image
classification with a small Vision
Transformer~\cite{dosovitskiy2020image} (4 layers, 192 dim, 4 heads,
patch size 4) and a RotNet auxiliary head (4-way rotation
prediction)~\cite{gidaris2018unsupervised}. The auxiliary loss uses the
same $\lambda = 0.05$ constant weight.

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Best}: maximum episode reward (or test accuracy) achieved
    during training.
    \item \textbf{Final50} (MARL) / \textbf{Final5} (vision): mean of the
    last 50 episodes (or last 5 epochs), capturing the policy's stable
    convergence level.
    \item \textbf{Collapse \%}: $({\mathrm{Best} - \mathrm{Final}}) /
    {\mathrm{Best}} \times 100$.
    \item We report mean $\pm$ population standard deviation over 5 random

\subsection{Causal Identification of the Mechanism}
\label{sec:mechanism}

The fix paths are consistent with two distinct stories: directional
interference driven by structured non-stationary aux targets, and a
generic aux-capacity-consumption alternative in which any aux head
attached to the shared encoder degrades late-training performance
regardless of what the aux gradient encodes. We rule out the latter
with two independent controls.

\textbf{Target-source test.} We hold aux capacity, architecture, and
the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway identical to Full and vary only
the \emph{source} of the aux target (\S\ref{sec:method_distinguishing}).
The capacity-consumption hypothesis predicts all three conditions to
be pathological. Table~\ref{tab:target_source} falsifies this:
frozen-policy targets partially recover (std $8.55 \to 4.52$, mean
within $\sim 3$ points of No Aux), and uniform random targets fully
recover the no-aux regime (Cohen's $d = -0.04$ vs No Aux, CI crossing
zero; the tightest cross-seed std in the paper). Structured
co-learning targets carry teammate-policy-specific information that
drifts across iterations, yielding an aux gradient direction that
shifts with teammate updates; frozen targets remove the drift while
retaining structured content; random targets remove both. Only the
structured-and-drifting combination degrades Final50 stability.

\subsection{Fix Paths, Given the Mechanism}
\label{sec:fixes}

Each of the three fix paths in Table~\ref{tab:ablation}~(Group~B)
drives $J_\pi \to 0$ in the encoder subspace (Pathway-null
prediction): $\lambda$-annealing decays the aux contribution,
stop-gradient severs it at the belief, critic-side placement routes
it away from the actor's encoder. All three land in Final50
$[471.60, 473.66]$, indistinguishable from No Aux by bootstrap CI
(Appendix~\ref{app:tables}); stop-gradient drops cross-seed std
from $8.55$ to $2.13$ ($4\times$ reduction).

both aux-ON cells (Full and No Attention) are pathological, both
aux-OFF cells are healthy, and the 2$\times$2 pattern lacks the
attention specificity seen on AA. Under the target-source reading,
this is the expected gradient across environments: as teammate-policy
drift and coordination difficulty grow, even mean-pool aux pathways
become disruptive; on sparser-coordination settings (MPE) the drift is
small enough that the aux is harmless, and in fully stationary
supervised learning (CIFAR-100) the drift is zero and the aux is
near-null. The bound in Proposition~\ref{prop:variance} formalizes
this scaling: late-training variance grows with the temporal variance
of the aux-gradient direction, which in turn grows with teammate
co-learning drift.

On SMAX, fix paths reduce cross-seed std from $0.46$ to $0.22$--$0.29$
but do not recover the mean ($10.54$--$10.63$ vs No Aux $11.15$); a
direct test halving $\lambda$ to $0.025$ worsens rather than improves
SMAX ($10.18 \pm 0.35$), a non-monotonic response we flag as open.

\subsection{Architecture and $\lambda$ Generalization}
\label{sec:arch_sweep}

A 60-run architecture sweep (Appendix~\ref{app:arch_sweep}) finds
the pathology reproduces with LSTM + cross-attention ($d=0.80$) and
GRU + additive attention ($d=1.01$); mean pooling is immune
($d=-0.30$); critic-side aux reverses the effect ($d=-0.79$). A
3-point $\lambda$ sweep (Appendix~\ref{app:lambda}) finds a sweet
spot at $\lambda{=}0.01$ rather than a monotonic threshold, and at
50M steps both $\lambda{=}0.01$ and $\lambda{=}0.05$ retain std $>7$,
so the pathology is not a transient of the 10M budget.

\subsection{Training-budget artifact (secondary).}
\label{sec:budget}
A 6-method benchmark at 10M steps (Appendix~\ref{app:tables}) shows
no method collapses at 10M; coordination collapse on AA drops from
$\sim$46\% at 500K to $<$3\% at 10M. The residual instability we
isolate is distinct from, and much smaller than, the widely reported
collapse.


% ============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{The mechanism, restated.}
The target-source test and the $8{\times}$ capacity sweep together
falsify capacity consumption: the pathway is necessary but not
sufficient. Cohen's $d$ grows with the speed at which teammate
policies drift (Table~\ref{tab:cross_env}): MPE's symmetric drift is
near-null; SMAX and AA are moderate; Cramped Room and Hanabi are
large, with Hanabi losing AA's clean $2{\times}2$ interaction because
partial information makes even mean-pool aux disruptive.

\paragraph{Relation to prior work.}
BEPAL~\cite{huang2025bepal}'s oracle (stationary) targets are
consistent with our principle. Dynamic Belief~\cite{zhai2023dynamic}
reports the same failure mode and fixes it via meta-learned dynamic
context; we identify the mechanism and show three gradient-pathway
interventions recover the no-aux regime without meta-learning.
Contemporaneous~\cite{weng2026exploration} finds a distinct
magnitude-driven pathology in parameter-shared DQN.



% ============================================================================
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================================

5 seeds throughout; 2 of 4 architecture-sweep attention configs
resolve the pathology at positive Cohen's $d$, Neither-vs-Full AA
CI crosses zero, the $\lambda$ sweep is 3 points, sample-efficiency
is AA-only, CIFAR-100 is single-seed, and Hanabi uses off-the-shelf
hyperparameters so absolute scores are low. AERIAL gradient
decomposition and a separate-encoder VABL control are future work.
Proposition~\ref{prop:variance} uses linearized dynamics, i.i.d.\
noise, and a first-order expansion in $\pi_j$; $J_\pi$ is not
closed-form for VABL.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================================

Structured non-stationary aux targets inject directional noise
dominating policy gradients near convergence; target-source and
capacity controls rule out alternatives; CIFAR-100 and MPE nulls are
the predicted boundary. Three gradient-pathway interventions recover
stability on Overcooked.

\section{Full Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}}
\label{app:proof}

Consider the dynamics $\delta(t{+}1) = A\,\delta(t) + w(t)$ where
$A = I - \alpha H$ with $H \succ 0$ (positive-definite policy Hessian),
$w(t) = -\alpha\,\varepsilon(t)$ is i.i.d.\ with $\mathbb{E}[w] = 0$
and $\mathrm{Cov}[w] = \alpha^2 \Sigma_\varepsilon$.

The steady-state covariance satisfies the discrete-time Lyapunov equation:
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma_\delta = A\,\Sigma_\delta\,A^T + \alpha^2\,\Sigma_\varepsilon.
\end{equation}

Since $H$ is symmetric, let $H = Q \Lambda Q^T$ with eigenvalues
$h_1 \leq h_2 \leq \cdots \leq h_d$. In the eigenbasis, $A$ is diagonal
with entries $a_i = 1 - \alpha h_i$, and the Lyapunov equation decouples:
\begin{equation}
    (\Sigma_\delta)_{ii} = \frac{\alpha^2\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}
                                 {1 - a_i^2}
    = \frac{\alpha^2\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}
           {2\alpha h_i - \alpha^2 h_i^2}.
\end{equation}

For standard learning rates $\alpha h_i \ll 1$, the denominator is
approximately $2\alpha h_i$, giving:
\begin{equation}
    (\Sigma_\delta)_{ii} \approx
    \frac{\alpha\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}{2 h_i}.
\end{equation}

The trace (total steady-state variance) is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\|\delta\|^2] = \mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\delta)
    \approx \frac{\alpha}{2} \sum_i
    \frac{(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}{h_i}
    \;\leq\; \frac{\alpha\,\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\varepsilon)}
                   {2\,h_{\min}},
\end{equation}
which establishes the bound in Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}. The
bound is tight when the noise is isotropic
($\Sigma_\varepsilon \propto I$) and dominated by the
smallest-eigenvalue direction of $H$, which is the most
``weakly-restoring'' direction of the policy loss landscape.

or mean pooling) independently while keeping all other hyperparameters fixed.
Each configuration is run with and without the auxiliary loss (5 seeds
each, 60 runs total). A MAAC-style variant places the auxiliary head on the
centralized critic instead of the actor.

The pathology appears whenever \emph{any} recurrence (GRU or LSTM) is paired
with \emph{any} learned attention mechanism (cross-attention or additive) and
a constant auxiliary loss on the actor. Mean pooling, which has no learned
attention parameters, is immune ($d = -0.30$). The MAAC-style
critic-auxiliary configuration reverses the effect ($d = -0.79$): the
auxiliary gradient flows through the critic rather than the actor's belief
encoder, so it corrupts value estimates (which are recomputed each epoch)
rather than the policy representation (which accumulates damage through
recurrence).

cannot be resolved against this intrinsic variance at $n=5$: the
Cohen's $d$ for Full vs No Aux is $-0.54$ with CI crossing zero.

refutation. Three interpretations are consistent with the data.
(a)~The VAE reparameterization adds training stochasticity that
dominates the $n=5$ pathology signal; a larger $n$ or longer training
would be required to resolve. (b)~The VAE's implicit regularization
partially mitigates the directional-interference pathology, leaving a
residual gap below the resolution of this test. (c)~The VAE belief is
a different enough architecture that the pathology has different
dynamics here; a VAE-specific study would be needed to characterize
them. The main paper does not rely on this experiment.

\section{Figure Accessibility}
\label{app:accessibility}

All figures in this paper are designed for accessibility across viewing
conditions. One author has color vision deficiency, which motivated a
systematic approach to figure design:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Color palette.} We use the IBM colorblind-safe palette
    (magenta, blue, purple, orange, gold, teal), which maintains perceptual
    distinctness under deuteranopia, protanopia, and tritanopia. The
    pathological configuration (Full VABL) uses magenta throughout all
    figures for consistent identification.

    \item \textbf{Redundant encoding.} Every data series is distinguished by
    at least two independent visual channels. Dot plots use distinct marker
    shapes (cross, square, diamond, circle). Bar charts use hatching patterns
    (diagonal, dots, backslash, crosshatch) in addition to color. Line plots
    use distinct line styles (solid, dashed, dash-dot, dotted) with large
    markers.

    \item \textbf{Grayscale readability.} All figures remain interpretable
    when printed in black-and-white. We verified this by converting each
    figure to grayscale and confirming that all data series remain
    distinguishable through shape, pattern, and line style alone.

    \item \textbf{Font consistency.} Figure text (axis labels, legends,
    annotations) uses the same serif font family as the paper body to
    maintain visual coherence. We avoid matplotlib's default sans-serif
    rendering.

    \item \textbf{No duplicate titles.} Figures use LaTeX captions only,
    with no redundant matplotlib titles baked into the image. This avoids
    visual clutter and ensures consistent typography.
\end{itemize}

We encourage the MARL community to adopt similar accessibility practices.
Approximately 8\% of males and 0.5\% of females have some form of color
vision deficiency, and many readers access papers in printed grayscale.
Relying solely on color to distinguish data series excludes these readers.
