\begin{abstract}
Attention-based belief encoders trained with constant-weight auxiliary
prediction losses are a widespread design pattern in cooperative
multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL). We identify a
directional gradient-interference pathology in this pattern under
co-learning non-stationarity. In our 10M-step reruns of the published
AERIAL architecture~\cite{phan2023attention} on Overcooked Asymmetric
Advantages, the 5-seed results exhibit a late-training drop: 4 of 5
seeds reach peak $\sim$482 and then drift down to Final50 $\sim$463
(drop $15.5 \pm 3.3$), while a fifth seed fails to converge at all
(all-seed aggregate: drop $12.6 \pm 6.6$). To isolate the mechanism
we construct VABL as a minimal architecture in the same class and run
a 7-configuration ablation across three environment families
(Overcooked, SMAX, MPE). The pathology manifests on Overcooked
(Cohen's $d$ up to $5.06$) and SMAX ($d = 1.26$) but not on the
simpler MPE (cooperative navigation), consistent with our theoretical
prediction of environment-dependent instability thresholds. Three
gradient-targeted interventions all sever the auxiliary-to-encoder
gradient pathway and all converge to the no-aux regime, isolating
that pathway as the necessary conduit for the instability. A gradient
decomposition on VABL reveals the auxiliary gradient is small in
magnitude ($<25\%$ of the policy gradient) but oscillates erratically
in direction, pointing to directional rather than magnitude-driven
interference. A 60-run architecture sweep reproduces the effect at positive Cohen's
$d$ in two of four attention-based actor configurations (GRU +
additive, LSTM + cross-attention), with the other two too
high-variance at $n=5$ to resolve an aux effect; mean pooling is
immune and critic-side auxiliary placement reverses the sign. We
additionally show that ``coordination collapse'' on Overcooked is
largely a training-budget artifact (collapse $<5\%$ at 10M steps). For
practitioners, in the configurations we tested, any one of three
gradient-pathway interventions is sufficient: $\lambda \leq 0.01$ or
annealing, stop-gradient on the belief into the auxiliary head, or
placing the auxiliary head on the centralized critic. Code, training
logs, and per-seed JSON results for all 350{+} runs are available at
\url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/aux-loss-considered-harmful}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Cooperative MARL on Overcooked is widely reported to collapse after
reaching strong peak reward during training: MAPPO by
76\%~\cite{yu2022surprising}, AERIAL by
100\%~\cite{phan2023attention}, with communication-based methods
showing similar instability~\cite{das2019tarmac, sukhbaatar2016commnet}.
These reports have motivated auxiliary prediction
losses~\cite{jaderberg2016reinforcement}, attention-based belief
encoders~\cite{phan2023attention}, and explicit communication channels
as stabilization mechanisms. The resulting design pattern, a shared
encoder trained jointly with a policy loss and a constant-weight
auxiliary prediction loss, is now widespread beyond MARL, appearing
in world models~\cite{hafner2023mastering}, multi-task
RL~\cite{jaderberg2016reinforcement}, and self-supervised
representation learning~\cite{schwarzer2021dataefficient}. We find
most of the reported Overcooked collapse to be a training-budget
artifact. In our sample-efficiency measurements (four VABL ablation
configurations, 5 seeds each, 80 runs), collapse drops from $\sim$46\%
at 500K environment steps to $<$6\% at 2.5M and $<$3\% at 10M, and
MAPPO, TarMAC, and CommNet all converge stably (collapse $\leq 3\%$)
by 10M.

\textbf{One architectural class still degrades at 10M steps.} In our
10M-step reruns of AERIAL~\cite{phan2023attention}, the 5-seed results
exhibit a striking late-training drop: four seeds reach a peak of
$\sim$482 and then drift down to Final50 $\sim$463 (drop $15.5 \pm
3.3$), while a fifth seed never converges, plateauing at $\sim$360
from roughly 25\% of training onward (5-seed aggregate drop $12.6 \pm
6.6$). This 10-20 point residual drop is a distinct, gradient-mediated
pathology of the architectural class, not the short-horizon collapse
reported in earlier literature.

\textbf{Isolating the mechanism.} To investigate this pathology under
controlled ablation, we construct \textbf{VABL} (Variational
Attention-based Belief Learning), a minimal architecture in the same
class. VABL is deliberately simpler than AERIAL: it has no learned
message channel, no hidden-state sharing, and a cleanly separable
auxiliary pathway, which lets us toggle the auxiliary loss and
intervene on its gradient flow without architectural changes. In our
7-configuration VABL ablation on Overcooked AA at 10M steps, the
attention + constant-aux configuration is the only one that exhibits
late-training instability. Best reward ($\sim$484) matches all
alternatives, but Final50 drops to $463 \pm 8.6$ with $2$--$5\times$
higher cross-seed variance. The pathology reproduces on a second
Overcooked layout (Cohen's $d = 5.06$ on Cramped Room) and on SMAX
($d = 1.26$) but not on MPE simple\_spread, consistent with our
theoretical prediction that the instability threshold depends on the
complexity of co-learning dynamics (Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}).

Three gradient-targeted interventions, \textbf{(i)}~$\lambda$-annealing,
\textbf{(ii)}~stop-gradient on the belief input to the auxiliary head,
and \textbf{(iii)}~both combined, converge to a single recovered regime
($470$--$474$), statistically equivalent to removing the auxiliary loss.
This convergent evidence causally implicates the auxiliary gradient flowing
into the attention-based encoder. A CIFAR-100 cross-domain test finds the
pathology absent in supervised learning, narrowing it to MARL's co-learning
non-stationarity.

Our contributions: \textbf{(1)}~identification of a directional
gradient-interference pathology in the attention + constant-auxiliary
design pattern under MARL co-learning, first observed in the published
AERIAL architecture~\cite{phan2023attention} and then isolated under
controlled ablation in a minimal test bed (VABL), including
environment-dependence (present on Overcooked and SMAX, absent on
MPE) and $\lambda$ sensitivity between $\lambda{=}0.01$ (stable) and
$\lambda{=}0.05$ (unstable); \textbf{(2)}~a theoretical analysis
(Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}) whose key bound scales with
$\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\varepsilon)/\lambda_{\min}(H)$, paired with a
gradient decomposition showing the auxiliary gradient is small in
magnitude ($<25\%$) but oscillates erratically in direction;
\textbf{(3)}~three gradient-targeted fix paths that all sever the
auxiliary-to-encoder gradient pathway and all recover to the no-aux
regime, yielding concrete design rules (\emph{$\lambda \leq 0.01$ or
anneal; stop-gradient on the belief into the auxiliary head; or
place the auxiliary head on the centralized critic instead of the
actor}), generalized in a 60-run architecture sweep
(Table~\ref{tab:arch_sweep}) across recurrence and attention variants;
\textbf{(4)}~evidence that the much-reported Overcooked ``coordination
collapse'' is largely a training-budget artifact, plus a reproducible
350{+}-run, 5-seed benchmark across three MARL environment families
with anonymized code, training logs, and per-run result files at
\url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/aux-loss-considered-harmful}.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Auxiliary losses in RL.}
UNREAL~\cite{jaderberg2016reinforcement} pioneered auxiliary tasks for
representation shaping; subsequent work uses world-model
losses~\cite{hafner2023mastering}, self-predictive
representations~\cite{schwarzer2021dataefficient}, and contrastive
objectives~\cite{laskin2020curl}.
Lyle et al.~\cite{lyle2021effect} provide a theoretical analysis of
how auxiliary tasks shape representation dynamics in TD-based deep RL,
and Du et al.~\cite{du2018adapting} and Lin et al.~\cite{lin2019adaptive}
both argue that constant auxiliary weights are suboptimal and propose
adaptive schemes (cosine-similarity gating and online weighting,
respectively) as remedies. In the MARL setting,
XP-MARL~\cite{xu2024xpmarl} frames an auxiliary prioritization signal
explicitly as a tool to manage co-learning non-stationarity. We
instead \emph{characterize} the pathology:
under MARL co-learning, constant weights destabilize attention-based
encoders at long horizons, and three distinct gradient interventions
converge to the same recovered regime.

\paragraph{Attention in MARL.}
AERIAL~\cite{phan2023attention} uses attention over teammate hidden states;
MAAC~\cite{iqbal2019actor} places attention in the centralized critic;
MAT~\cite{wen2022mat} uses an encoder-decoder for centralized policy
learning. None pair attention-based \emph{decentralized} belief encoding
with an explicit auxiliary loss, the specific combination our ablation
targets.

\paragraph{Stop-gradient in SSL.}
BYOL~\cite{grill2020bootstrap}, SimSiam~\cite{chen2021exploring}, and
DINO~\cite{caron2021emerging} use stop-gradient to prevent representation
collapse. Our stop-gradient fix path is analogous: it prevents the auxiliary
loss from destabilizing the belief encoder via gradient interference.

\paragraph{Gradient interference.}
PCGrad~\cite{yu2020gradient} projects conflicting task gradients;
GradNorm~\cite{chen2018gradnorm} balances task weights dynamically;
HyperMARL~\cite{tessera2025hypermarl} introduces agent-conditioned
hypernetworks to decouple cross-agent gradient flow in the actor
parameters. These methods address \emph{cross-task} or \emph{cross-agent}
interference: two losses (or two agents) at the same time step pulling
in opposite directions. Multi-timescale
learning~\cite{nekoei2023dealing} addresses MARL non-stationarity
differently, by slowing partner updates so that each agent sees a
quasi-stationary target.

None of these techniques directly targets the mechanism we identify.
PCGrad and GradNorm operate on the \emph{magnitudes} of concurrent task
gradients, but the auxiliary gradient in our pathology is already small
(magnitude ratio $<25\%$); the harm is in its \emph{direction}, which
oscillates from step to step under co-learning. HyperMARL decouples
gradients \emph{across agents}, but our pathology is intra-agent: the
auxiliary and policy objectives share the same actor's belief encoder.
Multi-timescale learning reduces the rate at which partner policies
drift but does not remove the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway, which
is what our three fix paths intervene on. Our finding is qualitatively
different from all of these: the interference is \emph{temporal} (the
auxiliary gradient is benign early but becomes destabilizing late in
training under co-learning non-stationarity), \emph{directional} rather
than magnitude-driven (the auxiliary gradient is small but erratically
oriented), and \emph{environment-dependent} (present on complex
coordination tasks, absent
on simpler ones). These distinctions are not captured by existing
gradient-surgery frameworks.

\paragraph{Coordination collapse.}
Overcooked~\cite{carroll2019utility} is widely used for evaluating
cooperative MARL~\cite{huh2024multiagent}, with prior reports of severe
degradation for MAPPO~\cite{yu2022surprising}, value
decomposition~\cite{rashid2020monotonic}, and communication
methods~\cite{das2019tarmac}. Cooperative MARL has long
been known to suffer named coordination pathologies, most prominently
relative overgeneralization~\cite{wei2016lenient}, where partners' random
exploration biases joint-action value estimates toward suboptima. Deep
RL more broadly exhibits late-training degradation through
mechanisms like capacity loss~\cite{lyle2022capacity}. Our 10M-step
evaluation reveals that most reported MARL ``collapse'' results reflect
insufficient training; the residual instability is a distinct,
gradient-mediated pathology.

\section{Background: Attention-Based Belief Learning}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================================

The design pattern we investigate, attention-based belief encoders
trained with constant-weight auxiliary prediction losses, appears in
multiple published methods including AERIAL (attention over teammate
hidden states, auxiliary recurrence loss)~\cite{phan2023attention}
and is structurally compatible with the broader family of world-model
and self-predictive representation architectures that use constant
auxiliary weights~\cite{hafner2023mastering, schwarzer2021dataefficient,
jaderberg2016reinforcement}. We construct \textbf{VABL} (Variational
Attention-based Belief Learning) as a minimal architecture in this
class, operating on cooperative Dec-POMDPs~\cite{oliehoek2016concise},
and chosen to be as small and cleanly ablatable as possible so that
attention, the auxiliary loss, and the gradient flow between them can
be manipulated independently.

\paragraph{Belief update.}
Each agent $i$ maintains a recurrent latent belief $b_t^i \in
\mathbb{R}^{d_h}$, updated at each timestep from (i) an observation
embedding $\phi_\theta(o_t^i) \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ and (ii) a social
context vector $c_t^i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_e}$ aggregated from observable
teammate actions:
\begin{equation}
    b_t^i = \mathrm{GRU}\left(
        [\phi_\theta(o_t^i) \| c_t^i],\; b_{t-1}^i
    \right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{GRU}$ denotes a gated recurrent unit~\cite{cho2014gru}.

\paragraph{Attention context.}
The social context is computed via multi-head attention~\cite{vaswani2017attention} over action embeddings
of visible teammates:
\begin{equation}
    c_t^i = \mathrm{MHA}\left(
        \mathrm{query} = W_q b_{t-1}^i, \quad
        \mathrm{keys/values} = \{\psi_\theta(a_{t-1}^j) + e_j\}_{j \neq i}
    \right),
\end{equation}
where $\psi_\theta$ is an action encoder, $e_j$ is a learnable identity
embedding for teammate $j$, and the attention is masked by a visibility
indicator $m_t^{i \leftarrow j} \in \{0,1\}$. When no attention is used
(the mean-pooling ablation), $c_t^i$ is the unweighted average of visible
teammate action embeddings.

\paragraph{Policy and auxiliary heads.}
The policy $\pi_\theta(a_t^i \mid b_t^i)$ is a linear head on the belief.
The auxiliary predictor $\hat{\pi}_\varphi(\cdot \mid b_t^i)$ is a 2-layer
MLP that predicts each visible teammate's next action from the current
belief, trained with cross-entropy:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}}^i = \mathbb{E}\left[
        \sum_{j \neq i} m_{t+1}^{i \leftarrow j}\,
        \mathrm{CE}(\hat{\pi}_\varphi(\cdot \mid b_t^i),\, a_{t+1}^j)
    \right].
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The total loss combines a PPO policy gradient~\cite{schulman2017ppo}, a centralized value loss, entropy
regularization, and the auxiliary loss:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{PPO}} +
    c_v \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{value}} +
    c_e \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{entropy}} +
    \lambda \sum_i \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{aux}}^i,
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = 0.05$ is the auxiliary loss weight. In the standard
configuration, $\lambda$ is held constant throughout training.

\paragraph{Gradient pathway.} The auxiliary loss gradient flows through the belief
$b_t^i$ and into the attention mechanism, the GRU, and the observation
encoder $\phi_\theta$. This is the gradient pathway that our ablation
(Section~\ref{sec:results}) identifies as the source of the late-training
instability.

\section{Theoretical Analysis: Directional Interference}
\label{sec:theory}
% ============================================================================

We develop a minimal analytical model that predicts the empirical findings.
The key insight is that the pathology arises not from the \emph{magnitude}
of the auxiliary gradient but from its \emph{directional variance} under
co-learning non-stationarity.

\paragraph{Gradient decomposition near convergence.}
Near a local optimum $\theta^*$, the policy loss is approximately quadratic
with Hessian $H$, giving policy gradient $\nabla_\theta L_{\mathrm{policy}}
= H(\theta - \theta^*)$, which shrinks as $\theta \to \theta^*$. The
auxiliary gradient $g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t) = \lambda \nabla_\theta
L_{\mathrm{aux}}(\theta; \pi_j^{(t)})$ depends on teammate $j$'s current
policy $\pi_j^{(t)}$, which is non-stationary under co-learning.

\paragraph{Noise decomposition.}
We decompose $g_{\mathrm{aux}}(t) = \bar{g}_{\mathrm{aux}} + \varepsilon(t)$
into a mean component and zero-mean noise with covariance $\Sigma_\varepsilon$.
The noise $\varepsilon(t)$ arises specifically from co-learning
non-stationarity~\cite{hernandezleal2017survey, nekoei2023dealing}: if
teammate $j$'s policy were frozen, $\varepsilon \to 0$. This
non-stationarity is a defining feature of cooperative MARL and more
broadly of any co-learning multi-agent system. Prior work has tackled
it through multi-timescale learning rather than directly through a
gradient-level analysis of how auxiliary objectives propagate into
shared encoders.

\paragraph{Stochastic dynamics.}
Centering around the effective fixed point $\theta^*_{\mathrm{eff}} =
\theta^* - H^{-1}\bar{g}_{\mathrm{aux}}$, the displacement $\delta(t) =
\theta(t) - \theta^*_{\mathrm{eff}}$ evolves as:
\begin{equation}
    \delta(t{+}1) = (I - \alpha H)\,\delta(t) - \alpha\,\varepsilon(t),
    \label{eq:dynamics}
\end{equation}
a linear stochastic system driven by the directional noise of the auxiliary
gradient.

Intuitively, given an auxiliary gradient with directional noise
$\Sigma_\varepsilon$ and a policy loss with minimum curvature
$\lambda_{\min}(H)$, the bound below says the late-training parameter
variance grows with the noise (numerator) and shrinks with the
curvature in the flattest direction (denominator): a flatter landscape
amplifies the noise. The proposition is \emph{descriptive}, not a
first-principles prediction: it relates two measurable quantities
under a simplifying assumption.

\begin{proposition}[Steady-state variance; descriptive linear model]
\label{prop:variance}
Consider the linearized dynamics of Eq.~\eqref{eq:dynamics} with
noise $\varepsilon(t)$ (Cov $= \Sigma_\varepsilon$, treated as
i.i.d.\ for tractability) and spectral radius $\rho(I - \alpha H) < 1$.
Given these primitives, the steady-state parameter variance satisfies:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\|\delta\|^2]
    \;\propto\;
    \frac{\alpha^2 \,\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\varepsilon)}
         {\lambda_{\min}(H)}.
    \label{eq:variance_bound}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\textit{Assumptions and scope.} This is a descriptive model, not a
first-principles prediction of the pathology. We do not derive
$\Sigma_\varepsilon$ from a concrete teammate-drift generative
process; we treat it as an empirical primitive measured in our
gradient decomposition (Figure~\ref{fig:gradients}). The quadratic
loss and i.i.d.\ noise assumptions are simplifications; real PPO
dynamics are nonlinear and the noise is correlated. We establish two
things about this model. First, a controlled synthetic experiment
that directly constructs a linear stochastic system with prescribed
$H$ and $\Sigma_\varepsilon$ recovers the variance bound exactly
(Appendix~\ref{app:synthetic}, Figure~\ref{fig:synthetic}), confirming
the algebra is correct. Second, the qualitative relationships implied
by Eq.~\eqref{eq:variance_bound}, namely that increasing
$\Sigma_\varepsilon$ or decreasing $\lambda_{\min}(H)$ worsens
stability, and that driving $\Sigma_\varepsilon \to 0$ recovers
stability, are consistent with the seven experimental observations
enumerated below. The model is useful because its qualitative
implications match the data, and because it explains why the fix
paths work: each severs the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway and
therefore drives the effective $\Sigma_\varepsilon$ toward zero in
the subspace that couples to the encoder.

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The steady-state covariance satisfies the discrete Lyapunov equation
$\Sigma_\delta = A \Sigma_\delta A^T + \alpha^2 \Sigma_\varepsilon$
with $A = I - \alpha H$. Diagonalizing yields
$\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\delta) = \alpha^2 \sum_i
\sigma_{\varepsilon,i}^2 / (2\alpha h_i - \alpha^2 h_i^2)$, dominated by
$\lambda_{\min}(H)$. Full derivation in Appendix~\ref{app:proof}.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Qualitative implications and empirical consistency.}
We list each qualitative implication of Eq.~\eqref{eq:variance_bound}
and the corresponding experimental observation. We emphasize that
these are consistencies, not \emph{a priori} predictions: the
observations identify directions in which the data agrees with the
descriptive model, and give us reasons to take the bound's structure
seriously even though its assumptions are simplifications.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Implication:} Attention amplifies $\Sigma_\varepsilon$
    (multiplicative belief-query coupling) $\Rightarrow$ Full VABL should
    have the highest variance. \textbf{Consistent with:}
    Table~\ref{tab:ablation}, std $8.55$ vs.\ $1.86$--$3.72$ for
    alternatives.
\item \textbf{Implication:} $\lambda \to 0$ sends $\Sigma_\varepsilon \to 0$
    $\Rightarrow$ annealing should recover stability.
    \textbf{Consistent with:} B\_anneal Final50 $473.66$ $\approx$
    A\_no\_aux $473.45$ (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}).
\item \textbf{Implication:} Stop-gradient sets $\Sigma_\varepsilon = 0$
    exactly in the encoder subspace $\Rightarrow$ should also recover.
    \textbf{Consistent with:} B\_stopgrad Final50 $471.60$, lowest std
    ($2.13$).
\item \textbf{Implication:} Fixed auxiliary targets (supervised) $\Rightarrow$
    $\Sigma_\varepsilon \to 0$ naturally $\Rightarrow$ no pathology.
    \textbf{Consistent with:} CIFAR-100 shows $<0.5$ point aux effect
    (Appendix~\ref{app:vision}).
\item \textbf{Implication:} Complex co-learning $\Rightarrow$ large
    $\Sigma_\varepsilon$ $\Rightarrow$ above threshold; simpler
    $\Rightarrow$ below.
    \textbf{Consistent with:} pathology present on Overcooked and SMAX
    (complex coordination); absent on MPE (simple navigation).
\item \textbf{Implication:} Smaller $\lambda$ $\Rightarrow$ smaller
    $\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\varepsilon)$ $\Rightarrow$ below threshold.
    \textbf{Consistent with:} $\lambda{=}0.01$ has std $1.67$ (stable);
    $\lambda{=}0.05$ has std $8.55$ (unstable) at the two values we
    tested.
\item \textbf{Implication:} The mechanism is directional
    ($\Sigma_\varepsilon$), not magnitude ($\|g_{\mathrm{aux}}\|$).
    \textbf{Consistent with:} Figure~\ref{fig:gradients} shows
    $\|g_{\mathrm{aux}}\|/\|g_{\mathrm{PPO}}\| < 0.25$ but
    $\cos(g_{\mathrm{PPO}}, g_{\mathrm{aux}})$ oscillates in
    $[-0.47, +0.33]$ (5 seeds).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Empirical gradient evidence.}
Figure~\ref{fig:gradients} plots the gradient decomposition over training
for the Full VABL configuration on Overcooked AA. The pattern is consistent
across all 5 seeds: the cosine similarity between PPO and auxiliary gradients
oscillates erratically in $[-0.47, +0.33]$ while the magnitude ratio stays
below $0.25$. The auxiliary gradient \emph{decreases} over training as the
predictor improves, yet the directional instability persists, confirming
the mechanism is directional noise, not magnitude-driven.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Ablation Matrix}

We run the VABL test bed in a $2 \times 2$ ablation over the two components that
define the architecture's distinctive design:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Attention} vs.\ \textbf{mean pooling} for the social
    context aggregation.
    \item \textbf{Auxiliary loss on} ($\lambda = 0.05$, constant) vs.\
    \textbf{auxiliary loss off} ($\lambda = 0$).
\end{itemize}
This yields four configurations: \textbf{Full} (attention + aux),
\textbf{No Attention} (mean pool + aux), \textbf{No Aux} (attention only),
and \textbf{Neither} (mean pool only).

\subsection{Fix-Path Interventions}

To test whether the observed pathology is gradient-mediated, we apply three
interventions to the Full configuration that modify only the gradient flow,
not the architecture or loss function:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Anneal}: linearly decay $\lambda$ from 0.05 to 0 over the
    first 50\% of training iterations. The auxiliary loss still fires during
    early training but phases out before late-training instability manifests.
    \item \textbf{Stop-gradient}: apply \texttt{stop\_gradient} to the belief
    $b_t^i$ before it enters the auxiliary head. The auxiliary loss still
    trains its own MLP weights but cannot propagate gradients into the belief
    encoder. This intervention affects \emph{only} the gradient flow.
    \item \textbf{Anneal + stop-gradient}: both interventions combined.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Environments}

We evaluate on three environment families to test generalization:

\textbf{Overcooked}~\cite{carroll2019utility}: two layouts, \emph{Asymmetric
Advantages} (asymmetric roles, 2 agents, horizon 400) and \emph{Cramped Room}
(spatial bottleneck). 25,024 episodes ($\approx$10M env steps), 64 vectorized
parallel environments via JAX \texttt{vmap}.

\textbf{MPE simple\_spread}~\cite{lowe2017multi}: cooperative navigation with
3 symmetric agents (5 discrete actions, obs dim 18, horizon 25). 100,000
episodes ($\approx$2.5M env steps).

\textbf{SMAX 3v3}~\cite{rutherford2023jaxmarl}: combat coordination with
3 allied agents against 3 heuristic enemies (obs dim 75, horizon 100).
50,000 episodes ($\approx$5M env steps). SMAX is the JAX reimplementation
introduced in JaxMARL, inspired by but distinct from the original
SMAC~\cite{samvelyan2019starcraft}; it tests a fundamentally different
coordination domain (tactical combat vs.\ cooking/navigation).

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against four methods spanning three paradigms:
\textbf{MAPPO}~\cite{yu2022surprising} (decentralized policy gradient),
\textbf{TarMAC}~\cite{das2019tarmac} (targeted explicit communication),
\textbf{CommNet}~\cite{sukhbaatar2016commnet} (broadcast communication), and
\textbf{AERIAL}~\cite{phan2023attention} (attention over teammate hidden
states). All baselines are reimplemented in JAX~\cite{bradbury2018jax} using the
JaxMARL framework~\cite{rutherford2023jaxmarl} and trained with the same
hyperparameters, vectorized environment wrapper, and evaluation protocol.

\subsection{Cross-Domain Test}

To test whether the pathology generalizes beyond MARL, we replicate the
$2 \times 2$ ablation on CIFAR-100~\cite{krizhevsky2009learning} image
classification with a small Vision
Transformer~\cite{dosovitskiy2020image} (4 layers, 192 dim, 4 heads,
patch size 4) and a RotNet auxiliary head (4-way rotation
prediction)~\cite{gidaris2018unsupervised}. The auxiliary loss uses the
same $\lambda = 0.05$ constant weight.

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Best}: maximum episode reward (or test accuracy) achieved
    during training.
    \item \textbf{Final50} (MARL) / \textbf{Final5} (vision): mean of the
    last 50 episodes (or last 5 epochs), capturing the policy's stable
    convergence level.
    \item \textbf{Collapse \%}: $({\mathrm{Best} - \mathrm{Final}}) /
    {\mathrm{Best}} \times 100$.
    \item We report mean $\pm$ population standard deviation over 5 random
    seeds for all MARL experiments, and Cohen's $d$~\cite{cohen1988statistical}
    for effect sizes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Architecture Generalization}
\label{sec:arch_sweep}

A 60-run architecture sweep (Table~\ref{tab:arch_sweep} in
Appendix~\ref{app:arch_sweep}) swaps recurrence and attention independently
on Overcooked AA. Three findings:
\textbf{(i)}~the pathology generalizes: LSTM + cross-attention ($d = 0.80$)
and GRU + additive attention ($d = 1.01$) both reproduce it;
\textbf{(ii)}~mean pooling is immune ($d = -0.30$), isolating learned
attention weights as the vulnerability;
\textbf{(iii)}~placing the auxiliary head on the centralized critic
(MAAC-style~\cite{iqbal2019actor}) \emph{reverses} the effect
($d = -0.79$): the pathology is specific to auxiliary gradients flowing
through the actor's shared belief encoder.

\paragraph{Design recommendation.}
Taking the sweep together with the fix-path results, we offer the
following recommendation for MARL practitioners: decentralized actor
architectures that combine learned attention with a constant-weight
auxiliary loss on the actor-side shared encoder may be at risk for
the late-training directional-interference pathology we document, in
the configurations we tested. Of the six sweep configurations, two
(GRU + additive, LSTM + cross-attention) showed the effect at
positive Cohen's $d$, two (GRU + self-attention, FF + cross-attention)
had intrinsic cross-seed variance too high to resolve an aux effect,
one (mean pooling) was immune, and one (critic-side aux) reversed the
sign. Within the resolvable cases, the pattern is consistent: any of
three interventions (mean pooling instead of learned attention,
critic-side instead of actor-side auxiliary placement, or severing
the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway via annealing or stop-gradient)
suffices to recover the no-aux regime.

\paragraph{Environment-dependent threshold and $\lambda$ sensitivity.}
We test on SMAX 3v3 (combat, 50K episodes) and MPE simple\_spread
(navigation, 100K episodes). On SMAX, the pathology \textbf{reproduces}:
No~Aux ($11.15 \pm 0.41$) beats Full ($10.60 \pm 0.46$), Cohen's
$d = 1.26$, matching Overcooked's direction. On MPE, the pathology is
\textbf{absent}: Full VABL ($-18.82 \pm 0.99$) performs comparably to
No~Attn ($-18.59 \pm 1.08$), with no penalty for the attention + aux
combination. This pattern, present on Overcooked and SMAX, absent on
MPE and in supervised learning (Appendix~\ref{app:vision}), matches
Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}'s environment-dependent threshold. We
interpret the contrast mechanistically: Overcooked AA and SMAX 3v3
both require precise temporal synchronization of asymmetric roles, so
teammate~$j$'s policy $\pi_j^{(t)}$ changes rapidly whenever either
agent restructures its behavior, amplifying the non-stationarity that
drives $\Sigma_\varepsilon$. MPE simple\_spread has symmetric,
distance-based objectives, so partner policies shift more slowly and
$\Sigma_\varepsilon$ remains below the instability threshold.

SMAX also reveals a nuance relative to Overcooked. On Overcooked, the
fix paths (anneal, stop-grad, combined) all converge to the no-aux
regime in both mean and variance. On SMAX, the fix paths reduce
variance (std $0.46 \to 0.22$--$0.29$) but do not fully recover the
mean (Final50 $10.54$--$10.63$ vs No~Aux $11.15$; Appendix~\ref{app:tables}).
This suggests that on SMAX the auxiliary loss is \emph{actively
harmful} in expectation, not merely destabilizing: even with the
gradient pathway severed, having trained with the auxiliary signal
for the first half of training appears to leave a residual performance
gap. We do not have a complete account of this distinction and flag
it as an open question. A $\lambda$ sweep confirms the Overcooked
threshold behavior: $\lambda{=}0.01$ is stable (std $1.67$) while
$\lambda{=}0.05$ is unstable (std $8.55$); at 50M steps both converge
with similar high variance, confirming the pathology does not
self-resolve (Appendix~\ref{app:sample_efficiency}).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Why co-learning non-stationarity matters.}
Our gradient decomposition (Figure~\ref{fig:gradients}) shows the auxiliary
gradient is small in magnitude ($<25\%$ of PPO) but oscillates erratically
in direction (cosine in $[-0.47, +0.33]$ across 5 seeds). Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}
formalizes this: steady-state variance scales with
$\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\varepsilon)$, which stays nonzero under co-learning.
In supervised learning, both magnitude and direction converge, so the
interference never arises. This is why the same aux + attention
configuration is harmless on CIFAR-100 (Appendix~\ref{app:vision}) but
damaging on Overcooked and SMAX: the mathematical precondition for
directional noise simply does not exist outside of a co-learning
multi-agent setting.

\paragraph{Why learned attention is the vulnerability.}
The architecture sweep (Table~\ref{tab:arch_sweep}) isolates the mechanism:
mean pooling is immune because it has no learned parameters for the
auxiliary gradient to corrupt, while the MAAC-style critic configuration is
safe because the auxiliary gradient flows through the \emph{critic} (which
only affects value estimates) rather than the \emph{actor's} belief encoder
(which directly corrupts the policy). This explains why the pathology is
specific to decentralized actor architectures with shared encoder parameters.
Critically, the effect survives independent swaps of recurrence type
(GRU and LSTM both reproduce it) and attention mechanism
(cross-attention and additive attention both reproduce it), so the
finding is unlikely to be a tuning artifact of any single architectural
choice.

\paragraph{Closing the loop on AERIAL.}
The signature documented in Section~\ref{sec:aerial_observation}
(peak reward unaffected, late-training drop of order 10--20 points,
large drop in 4/5 seeds, tightest-seed aggregate drop $15.5$ points) is
exactly what the directional-interference mechanism predicts for an
architecture in this class: a small-magnitude but directionally noisy
auxiliary gradient into a shared belief encoder, with no effect on
peak-time representation quality but a steady late-training drift.
AERIAL's own architecture tightly couples its auxiliary signal to the
attention mechanism and does not admit a clean aux-on / aux-off
ablation; our controlled study in VABL fills that gap. We conjecture
that any of our three fix paths applied to AERIAL would reduce its
late-training drop to the no-aux regime, but leave empirical
verification to future work because it requires a non-trivial AERIAL
fork.

\paragraph{Practical recommendations.}
Our analysis yields three gradient-pathway interventions, any one of
which is sufficient in our experiments, plus a methodological
recommendation. \textbf{(1)}~Use $\lambda \leq 0.01$ or anneal
$\lambda \to 0$ over the first half of training. The auxiliary
objective still contributes representation shaping early when partner
policies are random, and phases out before late-training instability
manifests. \textbf{(2)}~Apply stop-gradient on the belief before it
enters the auxiliary head. The auxiliary MLP still trains its own
weights, but the aux-to-encoder gradient pathway is severed at its
source. \textbf{(3)}~If auxiliary prediction is needed as an
architectural component rather than only a training-time regularizer,
place the auxiliary head on the centralized critic (MAAC-style)
rather than on the actor's shared belief encoder. Methodologically,
\textbf{(4)}~report at multiple training budgets; many published
coordination-collapse results reflect undertrained baselines rather
than architectural failure. Our stop-gradient fix is the direct
analogue of the mechanism used by BYOL, SimSiam, and
DINO~\cite{grill2020bootstrap, chen2021exploring, caron2021emerging}
to prevent auxiliary losses from corrupting a shared encoder in
self-supervised learning. Practitioners should pick whichever of
(1)--(3) best fits their codebase.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================================

\textbf{Scope:} The pathology is reproduced at positive Cohen's $d$
in two of four attention-based actor configurations in our architecture
sweep (GRU + additive, LSTM + cross-attention); the other two (GRU +
self-attention, FF + cross-attention) had intrinsic cross-seed variance
too high to resolve an aux effect. Testing on Hanabi~\cite{bard2020hanabi}
and at larger architectural scale would further map the boundary.

\textbf{AERIAL verification:} Our mechanism predicts AERIAL's
late-training drop, but direct gradient decomposition on AERIAL
requires a non-trivial fork of the published codebase; we leave
empirical verification of the fix paths on AERIAL itself to future
work. The 5-seed AERIAL aggregate in
Section~\ref{sec:aerial_observation} includes one seed that failed
to converge; the paper's quantitative claims report both the 4 healthy
seeds and the full 5-seed aggregate so readers can judge robustness
to the exclusion.

\textbf{Intra-actor control:} The cleanest control for the
``shared encoder'' claim would be a VABL variant with a separate
(non-shared) auxiliary encoder but otherwise identical architecture.
Our architecture sweep includes only the stronger MAAC-style critic-aux
reversal; a dedicated separate-encoder control is left for future
work.

\textbf{Statistical power:} All MARL experiments use 5 seeds per cell.
Cohen's $d$ values reported in Tables 2 and 3 should be interpreted
as point estimates at $n=5$; bootstrap confidence intervals are
provided in Appendix~\ref{app:tables} for both the headline Asymmetric
Advantages ablation and the Cramped Room cross-layout comparison.
The Neither vs Full comparison has a CI that crosses zero on
Asymmetric Advantages; our qualitative conclusion rests on the six
other pairwise comparisons that resolve cleanly.

\textbf{Narrow load-bearing claims:} The $\lambda$-threshold framing
is based on two values ($\lambda{=}0.01$ stable, $\lambda{=}0.05$
unstable); we do not report intermediate values and therefore
describe the effect as a range, not a sharp threshold. The
``coordination collapse is a training-budget artifact'' reframing is
based on sample-efficiency curves measured on Overcooked Asymmetric
Advantages only; replicating these curves on other layouts would
strengthen the claim.

\textbf{Theory:} Proposition~\ref{prop:variance} uses linearized
dynamics and i.i.d. noise assumptions; a stochastic-approximation
analysis under correlated, nonlinear PPO dynamics would be tighter.
The synthetic verification in Appendix~\ref{app:synthetic} confirms
the math is correct under the stated assumptions, not that the
assumptions are faithful to real PPO training; the empirical gradient
decomposition in Section~\ref{sec:theory} is the closest we come to
measuring the noise directly.

\textbf{Performance:} Communication methods (TarMAC, CommNet) achieve
$\sim$65\% higher reward on Overcooked Asymmetric Advantages; our
contribution is the pathology and its fixes, not a new
state-of-the-art architecture. The benchmark in Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}
includes these baselines as evidence for the training-budget artifact
framing, not as controls for the design-pattern pathology itself.


% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================================

We identify a directional gradient-interference pathology affecting
attention-based belief encoders trained with constant-weight auxiliary
prediction losses, first observed in the published AERIAL architecture
on Overcooked at 10M steps and then isolated under controlled ablation
in a minimal test bed (VABL). The pathology leaves peak reward
untouched but widens cross-seed spread by $2$--$5\times$ and drops
Final50 by 10--20 points in the configurations we resolve. It is
specific to MARL's co-learning non-stationarity (absent in supervised
CIFAR-100), reproduces in two of four attention-based actor variants in
our 60-run architecture sweep, and is absent with mean pooling or
reversed on critic-side auxiliary placement. Three gradient-targeted
interventions all sever the auxiliary-to-encoder gradient pathway and
all converge to the no-aux regime, identifying that pathway as the
necessary conduit. We additionally show that the much-reported
Overcooked ``coordination collapse'' is largely a training-budget
artifact at 10M steps. We release a 350{+}-run benchmark and recommend
$\lambda$-annealing, stop-gradient, or critic-side auxiliary placement
for any architecture in this design pattern.

\section{Full Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}}
\label{app:proof}

Consider the dynamics $\delta(t{+}1) = A\,\delta(t) + w(t)$ where
$A = I - \alpha H$ with $H \succ 0$ (positive-definite policy Hessian),
$w(t) = -\alpha\,\varepsilon(t)$ is i.i.d.\ with $\mathbb{E}[w] = 0$
and $\mathrm{Cov}[w] = \alpha^2 \Sigma_\varepsilon$.

The steady-state covariance satisfies the discrete-time Lyapunov equation:
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma_\delta = A\,\Sigma_\delta\,A^T + \alpha^2\,\Sigma_\varepsilon.
\end{equation}

Since $H$ is symmetric, let $H = Q \Lambda Q^T$ with eigenvalues
$h_1 \leq h_2 \leq \cdots \leq h_d$. In the eigenbasis, $A$ is diagonal
with entries $a_i = 1 - \alpha h_i$, and the Lyapunov equation decouples:
\begin{equation}
    (\Sigma_\delta)_{ii} = \frac{\alpha^2\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}
                                 {1 - a_i^2}
    = \frac{\alpha^2\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}
           {2\alpha h_i - \alpha^2 h_i^2}.
\end{equation}

For standard learning rates $\alpha h_i \ll 1$, the denominator is
approximately $2\alpha h_i$, giving:
\begin{equation}
    (\Sigma_\delta)_{ii} \approx
    \frac{\alpha\,(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}{2 h_i}.
\end{equation}

The trace (total steady-state variance) is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}[\|\delta\|^2] = \mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\delta)
    \approx \frac{\alpha}{2} \sum_i
    \frac{(\Sigma_\varepsilon)_{ii}}{h_i}
    \;\leq\; \frac{\alpha\,\mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_\varepsilon)}
                   {2\,h_{\min}},
\end{equation}
which establishes the bound in Proposition~\ref{prop:variance}. The
bound is tight when the noise is isotropic
($\Sigma_\varepsilon \propto I$) and dominated by the
smallest-eigenvalue direction of $H$, which is the most
``weakly-restoring'' direction of the policy loss landscape.

or mean pooling) independently while keeping all other hyperparameters fixed.
Each configuration is run with and without the auxiliary loss (5 seeds
each, 60 runs total). A MAAC-style variant places the auxiliary head on the
centralized critic instead of the actor.

{\footnotesize $^\ddagger$Both conditions exhibit high intrinsic cross-seed variance ($\sigma > 145$) in both the +aux and $-$aux cells, making the aux effect undetectable.}
\end{table}

The pathology appears whenever \emph{any} recurrence (GRU or LSTM) is paired
with \emph{any} learned attention mechanism (cross-attention or additive) and
a constant auxiliary loss on the actor. Mean pooling, which has no learned
attention parameters, is immune ($d = -0.30$). The MAAC-style
critic-auxiliary configuration reverses the effect ($d = -0.79$): the
auxiliary gradient flows through the critic rather than the actor's belief
encoder, so it corrupts value estimates (which are recomputed each epoch)
rather than the policy representation (which accumulates damage through
recurrence).

\section{Figure Accessibility}
\label{app:accessibility}

All figures in this paper are designed for accessibility across viewing
conditions. One author has color vision deficiency, which motivated a
systematic approach to figure design:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Color palette.} We use the IBM colorblind-safe palette
    (magenta, blue, purple, orange, gold, teal), which maintains perceptual
    distinctness under deuteranopia, protanopia, and tritanopia. The
    pathological configuration (Full VABL) uses magenta throughout all
    figures for consistent identification.

    \item \textbf{Redundant encoding.} Every data series is distinguished by
    at least two independent visual channels. Dot plots use distinct marker
    shapes (cross, square, diamond, circle). Bar charts use hatching patterns
    (diagonal, dots, backslash, crosshatch) in addition to color. Line plots
    use distinct line styles (solid, dashed, dash-dot, dotted) with large
    markers.

    \item \textbf{Grayscale readability.} All figures remain interpretable
    when printed in black-and-white. We verified this by converting each
    figure to grayscale and confirming that all data series remain
    distinguishable through shape, pattern, and line style alone.

    \item \textbf{Font consistency.} Figure text (axis labels, legends,
    annotations) uses the same serif font family as the paper body to
    maintain visual coherence. We avoid matplotlib's default sans-serif
    rendering.

    \item \textbf{No duplicate titles.} Figures use LaTeX captions only,
    with no redundant matplotlib titles baked into the image. This avoids
    visual clutter and ensures consistent typography.
\end{itemize}

We encourage the MARL community to adopt similar accessibility practices.
Approximately 8\% of males and 0.5\% of females have some form of color
vision deficiency, and many readers access papers in printed grayscale.
Relying solely on color to distinguish data series excludes these readers.
